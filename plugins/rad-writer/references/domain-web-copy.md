# Web Copy Writing Reference

## Headline Formulas

### Benefit-Driven Headlines
Lead with what the reader *gets*, not what the product *does*.
- **Do this**: "Ship 3x faster without sacrificing code quality"
- **Not that**: "Advanced CI/CD Pipeline Management Tool"
- **Do this**: "Stop losing customers at checkout"
- **Not that**: "E-commerce Checkout Optimization Solution"

The formula: `[Desirable outcome] + [without/in/by] + [timeframe or removed objection]`
- "Build production-ready APIs in an afternoon"
- "Grow your email list without annoying pop-ups"
- "Cut your AWS bill by 40% — without touching your infrastructure"

### Problem-Agitation Headlines
Name the pain. Make the reader nod.
- "Tired of debugging CSS that works on your machine but breaks in production?"
- "Your landing page isn't converting because visitors don't trust you yet"
- "Still manually updating spreadsheets every Monday morning?"

The formula: `[Name the pain] + [imply the cost of inaction]`

### The 4U Formula for Headlines
Every headline should score on four dimensions: **Useful** (does it promise value?), **Urgent** (is there a reason to read now?), **Unique** (is the angle fresh?), **Ultra-specific** (does it name exact outcomes, numbers, or details?). Rate each U from 1-4 and aim for a total score of 12+. A headline that scores 4/4 on useful but 1/4 on unique is still forgettable.

### Specificity Headlines
Specific numbers and details beat vague claims every time.
- **Do this**: "How we reduced page load time from 4.2s to 0.8s"
- **Not that**: "How to make your website faster"
- **Do this**: "The 3-email sequence that books 47% more discovery calls"
- **Not that**: "Improve your email outreach"

Odd numbers outperform even numbers in testing. "7 ways" beats "6 ways." "143% increase" is more credible than "150% increase."

### Service Provider Headline Formula
For consultancies, agencies, and freelancers: `[what you do] + [who it's for] + [what changes for them]`
- "Brand identity for SaaS startups that want to look like they've been around for 10 years"
- "Tax strategy for freelancers who want to keep more of what they earn"

### Headlines to Avoid
- "Welcome to [Company]" — nobody cares about a welcome; they care about what you can do for them.
- "The Ultimate Guide to X" — overused to the point of triggering skepticism.
- "Introducing [Product Name]" — the name means nothing to someone who doesn't know you yet.
- "Revolutionizing X" / "Disrupting Y" — show, don't claim.
- Puns that sacrifice clarity for cleverness: "Lettuce help you grow" for a gardening SaaS.

## Landing Page Structure and Section Sequencing

### The Proven Sequence

1. **Hero Section**: Headline (benefit-driven) + subheadline (how or proof) + primary CTA + hero image/demo
2. **Social Proof Bar**: Logos, customer count, or a single powerful testimonial. Placed high to establish credibility before asking for trust.
3. **Problem Section**: Articulate the reader's pain better than they can. When you describe their problem accurately, they trust you have the solution.
4. **Solution Section**: Show (don't just tell) how your product solves the problem. Screenshots, short demo, before/after.
5. **Features/Benefits**: 3-4 key features, each paired with a benefit. Feature = what it does. Benefit = why the reader cares.
6. **Social Proof (expanded)**: Testimonials, case studies, metrics. Specific > generic. "Reduced our onboarding time from 3 weeks to 2 days" beats "Great product!"
7. **Objection Handling**: FAQ section or dedicated blocks that address the top 3-5 reasons someone *wouldn't* buy.
8. **Final CTA**: Repeat the primary CTA. Restate the key benefit. Reduce friction ("Free trial", "No credit card required", "Cancel anytime").

### Section Sequencing Principles
- **Above the fold**: The hero section must communicate *what you do*, *for whom*, and *why it matters* without scrolling.
- **Credibility before ask**: Place social proof before feature details. Trust comes first, interest follows.
- **Escalating commitment**: The page should mirror the buyer's mental journey — problem recognition, solution awareness, product evaluation, decision.
- **Multiple CTAs**: Place CTAs after every major section, not just at the end. The reader might be ready to convert at any point.

## CTA (Call to Action) Writing

### High-Converting CTA Patterns
- **Action + outcome**: "Start my free trial" (not "Submit")
- **First person outperforms**: "Start My Free Trial" beats "Start Your Free Trial" — first-person creates psychological ownership before the click. The reader mentally rehearses the action as *theirs*.
- **Reduce friction**: "Try free for 14 days — no credit card" beats "Sign up"
- **Create urgency (honestly)**: "Join 2,400 teams already using [product]" implies momentum without fake scarcity
- **"Risk Reversal" microcopy**: Place friction-reducers directly beneath CTAs — "No credit card required," "Cancel anytime," "Free forever for small teams." These eliminate the objection at the exact moment it forms.

### CTA Anti-Patterns
| Weak CTA | Why it fails | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Submit" | Zero motivation. Sounds like homework. | "Get my results" |
| "Click here" | Meaningless. Also bad for accessibility. | "Download the template" |
| "Learn more" | Low commitment, but also low value signal. | "See how it works" |
| "Buy now" | Too aggressive on a first-visit landing page. | "Start free trial" |
| "Sign up for our newsletter" | Nobody wants a newsletter. They want what's in it. | "Get weekly conversion tips" |
| "Contact us" | Vague. Who am I contacting, and about what? | "Talk to an engineer" / "Book a demo" |

### Button Copy Rules
- 2-5 words. Button labels are not sentences.
- Start with a verb: Get, Start, Join, Download, Try, See, Build.
- Pair with a friction-reducer beneath: "No credit card required" / "Free forever for small teams" / "Takes 30 seconds."
- Avoid jargon in buttons: "Provision your instance" is not a CTA normal humans respond to.

## Product Descriptions: Feature-to-Benefit Translation

### The FAB Framework (Feature -> Advantage -> Benefit)
Translate features through two levels: what it *does* (feature), what that *enables* (advantage), and what it *means for the reader's life* (benefit). Example: "Automated workflows" (feature) -> "saves 4 hours per week" (advantage) -> "leave the office by 6 PM" (benefit). Most copy stops at the advantage. The benefit is where the emotional connection lives.

### The Translation Framework
Every feature can be expressed as: `[Feature] so you can [benefit] without [pain it eliminates]`

| Feature | Bad description | Good description |
|---|---|---|
| Real-time sync | "Data synchronizes in real time across all nodes" | "Your whole team sees changes the moment they happen — no more working from stale data" |
| One-click deploy | "Single-click deployment functionality" | "Ship to production in one click. No SSH, no scripts, no 'it works on my machine'" |
| 256-bit encryption | "Enterprise-grade AES-256 encryption" | "Your data is encrypted with the same standard banks use. Nobody sees it but you." |
| API access | "Full REST API with comprehensive documentation" | "Build exactly what you need on top of our platform. Every feature is API-accessible." |

### Product Description Mistakes
- **Feature stacking without benefits**: "Includes real-time sync, custom dashboards, role-based access, webhook integration, and SSO." Great — but *why should I care about any of those?*
- **Jargon as a substitute for clarity**: "Leverage our ML-powered NLP pipeline for enhanced sentiment analysis." Translation: "Understand how your customers feel — automatically."
- **Describing the technology instead of the outcome**: "Built on a distributed microservices architecture" tells the reader about your stack, not their life.
- **Identical descriptions across a competitor set**: If your description could appear on three competitors' sites unchanged, it says nothing distinctive.
- **"Thesaurus Syndrome"**: Stacking adjectives without concrete value — "powerful, innovative, cutting-edge, seamless solution." Each adjective dilutes the others. Replace the whole stack with one falsifiable fact.
- **"Point, Don't Talk" rule**: Replace adjectives with visual, falsifiable facts. Instead of "our blazing-fast platform," write "pages load in 0.8 seconds." Instead of "beautiful design," show a screenshot. If a claim can't be tested or visualized, it's just noise.

## Microcopy

### Button Labels
- Use specific verbs that describe what happens next: "Download PDF", "Create account", "Send message"
- Avoid ambiguity: "Go" could mean anything. "Continue to checkout" is clear.
- Match the user's mental model: "Add to cart" (not "Purchase item for later transaction processing")

### Form Labels and Placeholders
- Labels should describe the input: "Work email" (not "Email Address*")
- Placeholders show format, not instructions: "you@company.com" (not "Enter your email address here")
- Never use placeholder text as the only label — it disappears on focus, and users forget what field they're filling in.
- Required field indicators: use "(optional)" on optional fields rather than asterisks on required ones (most fields are required).

### Error Messages
- **Do this**: "That email is already registered. Want to log in instead?" (identifies problem, offers solution)
- **Not that**: "Error: Invalid input" (meaningless)
- **Do this**: "Password needs at least 8 characters and one number. You're at 6 characters." (specific, helpful)
- **Not that**: "Password does not meet requirements" (which requirements?)
- Replace generic errors with format-showing examples: instead of "Invalid input," write "Enter email like: you@email.com"
- Never blame the user: "You entered an invalid email" vs "That doesn't look like an email address — check for typos."
- Place errors inline, adjacent to the problem field, not in a banner at the top of the page.

### Empty States
- Don't leave empty screens blank. Use them to guide: "No projects yet. Create your first one to get started."
- **Empty states as engagement**: Turn dead ends into action moments. "Upload your first file" is more compelling than a blank screen with an icon. The best empty states teach the product's value by previewing what success looks like.
- Show what the screen *will* look like with content (skeleton, example, illustration).
- Make the CTA obvious: one prominent button to create/add/import.

### Loading and Wait States
- "Loading..." is the minimum. "Loading your dashboard..." is better. "Crunching 3 months of data..." is best.
- For long waits (>3 seconds), show progress or explain what's happening.
- "This usually takes about 10 seconds" manages expectations better than a spinning wheel.

### Success Messages
- Confirm *what* happened: "Your report has been emailed to team@company.com" (not just "Success!")
- Suggest a next step: "Profile updated. Want to set up two-factor authentication next?"
- Be proportional. Don't celebrate a form submission with confetti and a full-screen modal.

## Eugene Schwartz Awareness Levels

### The Five Levels
Understanding where your reader sits determines everything about your copy:

**Level 1: Unaware**
They don't know they have a problem. Your copy must educate before it sells.
- Lead with the problem/pain, not the solution.
- Content marketing, not landing pages, works at this stage.
- Example: An article titled "Why your development team is slower than it should be" for a team that hasn't identified speed as an issue.

**Level 2: Problem-Aware**
They know the problem but don't know solutions exist.
- Agitate the problem. Show the cost of inaction.
- Then introduce the *category* of solution.
- Example: "Deployment bottlenecks cost engineering teams an average of 4 hours per week. Here's why — and what high-performing teams do differently."

**Level 3: Solution-Aware**
They know solutions exist but haven't found yours.
- Lead with your differentiator. Why your approach is better, not just that it exists.
- Comparison pages, "how it works" content, and specific benefit claims work here.
- Example: "Unlike traditional CI tools, [Product] doesn't require YAML configuration. Connect your repo and deploy in 2 minutes."

**Level 4: Product-Aware**
They know your product but haven't bought. They're evaluating.
- Remove objections. Provide social proof. Offer a risk-free trial.
- Pricing pages, case studies, and FAQ sections are critical at this stage.
- Example: "Join 2,400 teams who've cut their deploy time in half. Start free — no credit card required."

**Level 5: Most Aware**
They know your product and almost want it. They need a nudge.
- Lead with the offer: discount, bonus, urgency, or simplified next step.
- Don't re-explain the product. Just make it easy to say yes.
- Example: "Your free trial starts now. We'll have you deployed in under 5 minutes."

### Matching Copy to Awareness Level
- The most common mistake is writing Level 4-5 copy (product features, pricing) for a Level 1-2 audience (people who don't know they have the problem yet).
- Homepage copy should target Level 2-3 (most first-time visitors).
- Retargeting ads should target Level 4-5 (they've seen you before).
- Blog content targets Level 1-2 (education and problem awareness).

## Brand Voice Frameworks

### The 3x3 Voice Framework
Define your brand voice with three adjectives, each with a "this, not that" boundary:

| Voice trait | This | Not that |
|---|---|---|
| Confident | We state opinions clearly | We're arrogant or dismissive |
| Friendly | We write like a smart colleague | We're unprofessional or try-hard casual |
| Clear | We use plain language | We're simplistic or condescending |

### Voice Application Across Contexts
The same brand voice shifts across contexts — it doesn't disappear:

| Context | Confident | Friendly | Clear |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homepage headline | "The deployment tool your team deserves" | — | "Deploy in 2 minutes. No config files." |
| Error message | — | "Oops, that didn't work. Here's what to try." | "Upload failed: file exceeds 10MB limit." |
| Pricing page | "Simple pricing. No surprises." | "Questions? Our team actually answers." | "$49/month. Includes everything. No tiers." |
| Changelog | "We fixed the thing that was driving you nuts." | "This one's for the 47 people who reported it." | "Fixed: CSV export now handles Unicode correctly." |

### Common Brand Voice Mistakes
- **Voice doc exists but nobody follows it**: Voice guidelines only work if they include enough examples that a new writer can pattern-match, not just adjectives.
- **Voice that's indistinguishable from competitors**: If you swap your name for a competitor's and the copy still works, your voice isn't distinctive.
- **Forced quirk**: Trying to be Mailchimp when you're a healthcare compliance platform. Voice should be authentic to the company's actual personality.
- **Inconsistency across surfaces**: The homepage is playful but the documentation is robotic. The tweet is casual but the email is formal. Voice should flex, not fracture.

## Context-Gathering Questions

Before writing web copy, ask:
1. **Who is the target reader?** Job title, pain points, what they've already tried, what they're comparing you to.
2. **What awareness level are they at?** (Schwartz levels 1-5). This determines whether you lead with problem, solution, or offer.
3. **What is the single desired action?** Every page has one primary conversion goal. What is it?
4. **What are the top 3 objections?** What would make someone *not* take that action? The copy must address these.
5. **What proof do you have?** Testimonials, case studies, metrics, logos. Social proof is not optional.
6. **What's the brand voice?** Three adjectives with boundaries. Or: link to an existing voice doc.
7. **Where is this copy appearing?** Homepage, product page, landing page for a specific campaign, email? Each has different conventions.
8. **What's the competitive landscape?** What are competitors saying? How do you differentiate?

## Quality Criteria for Web Copy Review

- [ ] The headline communicates a benefit, not just a feature or company name
- [ ] Above-the-fold content answers: what is this, who is it for, and why should I care
- [ ] Every feature is paired with a benefit (the "so you can..." test)
- [ ] CTAs use specific action verbs and reduce friction
- [ ] Social proof is specific and credible (not "Customers love us!")
- [ ] Copy matches the reader's awareness level
- [ ] Jargon is eliminated or explained
- [ ] Microcopy (buttons, forms, errors, empty states) is helpful, not generic
- [ ] The page has a single clear primary action — not five competing CTAs
- [ ] Brand voice is consistent across all elements on the page
- [ ] Copy passes the "swap test" — would this work for a competitor's product? If yes, it's too generic

## AI Pattern Considerations

### Common AI Tells in Web Copy
- **Superlative stacking**: "The most powerful, comprehensive, intuitive, and innovative platform." Real copywriters pick *one* claim and prove it.
- **"Whether you're...or..." constructions**: "Whether you're a startup founder or an enterprise CTO..." AI loves this inclusive-but-vague framing. Real copy picks a reader and speaks directly to them.
- **Buzzword density**: "Leverage AI-powered insights to unlock data-driven decision-making." Every word is a buzzword. None communicate anything concrete.
- **Perfect symmetry in feature lists**: Three features, each with exactly two sentences of description, each starting with a verb. Real feature sections have natural variation.
- **No specificity in social proof**: "Trusted by thousands of companies worldwide." Real copy says "Trusted by 2,400 teams including Stripe, Notion, and Linear."
- **CTAs that describe rather than motivate**: "Begin Your Journey" / "Unlock Your Potential" — AI-generated CTAs tend toward the aspirational and abstract rather than the concrete and actionable.
- **Excessive empathy in problem sections**: "We understand how frustrating it can be when..." Real problem-agitation is blunter: "Your deploy takes 45 minutes. Your competitors' takes 3."
- **Over-explanation**: AI explains things that should be shown. A screenshot or demo GIF replaces 200 words of product description.

### Making AI-Assisted Web Copy Sound Human
- Replace every abstraction with a concrete number, name, or example.
- Cut "comprehensive", "robust", "cutting-edge", "seamless", "world-class", and "state-of-the-art" — they are never the best word for what you mean.
- Make at least one claim that a competitor *can't* make. If they can all say it, it's not differentiation.
- Read each CTA and ask: "Would I click this?" If the answer requires imagining a very generous reader, rewrite.
- Add imperfection: a slightly casual phrase, a dash-interrupted thought, a fragment. Perfect polish reads as machine-generated.
- Verify every testimonial is attributed to a real person with a real title. "Sarah M., Marketing Director" is more credible than "Satisfied Customer."
