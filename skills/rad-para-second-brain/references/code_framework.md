# The CODE Framework — Complete Reference

## Overview

CODE stands for **Capture, Organize, Distill, and Express**. It is the backbone of the Building a Second Brain methodology — a map for navigating endless streams of information and turning what you consume into creative output and tangible results.

## Step 1: Capture — Keep What Resonates

Act as a curator of your information diet. Instead of passively hoarding everything, save only the ideas, quotes, and insights that truly resonate on an intuitive level.

### The 4 Capture Criteria

When unsure whether to save something, ask:
1. **Is it inspiring?** Does it resonate emotionally or creatively?
2. **Is it useful?** Could it help with a current or future project?
3. **Is it personal?** Does it relate to your own experiences or reflections?
4. **Is it surprising?** Does it contradict or expand your existing understanding?

If it meets at least one criterion, capture it.

### The 12 Favorite Problems

Inspired by Nobel Prize-winning physicist Richard Feynman, maintain a list of approximately 12 open-ended questions you are constantly exploring. These range from practical to grand:

**Examples:**
- "How can I make it a habit to exercise every day?"
- "How can we make society fairer?"
- "What does it mean to be a good leader?"
- "How can I make decisions with more confidence?"
- "How can I balance creativity with consistency?"

**How to use them:**
- Whenever you encounter new information, test it against your 12 problems
- If it provides an answer, a new angle, or a spark of insight for any of them, capture it
- This ensures you are capturing highly relevant material rather than random content
- Review and update your problems periodically as your life evolves

### The 12 Favorite Problems Workshop

Use this guided exercise to create or refresh your list. Work through it one question
at a time:

**Step 1: Brain dump (5 minutes)**
Write down every question, curiosity, challenge, or aspiration you can think of.
Don't filter — aim for 20-30 raw questions across all areas of your life:
- Career and professional growth
- Health and well-being
- Relationships and community
- Creative projects and hobbies
- Financial goals
- Learning and personal development
- Contribution and impact

**Step 2: Refine into open-ended questions (5 minutes)**
Rewrite each item as a genuine question starting with "How can I...?" or "What...?"
- Bad: "Get promoted" (this is a goal, not a problem)
- Good: "How can I develop leadership skills that make me indispensable?"
- Bad: "Read more books" (this is a habit)
- Good: "How can I extract more actionable insight from what I read?"

**Step 3: Select your 12 (5 minutes)**
Pick the 12 that energize you most. Criteria:
- You'd be excited to find an answer
- They're broad enough to last months or years
- They span different areas of your life (not all work, not all personal)
- They're specific enough to recognize a relevant answer when you see one

**Step 4: Test the filter**
Think of the last 3 things you saved or bookmarked. Do any relate to your 12 problems?
If not, either your captures are unfocused or your problems don't reflect your actual
interests. Adjust accordingly.

**Maintenance:** Review your 12 problems during monthly reviews. Replace problems that
no longer resonate. It's normal for 2-3 to change each quarter.

### Capture Tools and Techniques

- **Read-later apps** (Instapaper, Pocket) for online articles
- **Ebook highlight export** (Kindle, Apple Books) for book passages
- **Voice transcription apps** for stray thoughts and ideas
- **Web clipper** browser extensions for web pages
- **Screenshot tools** for visual inspiration
- **Quick capture note** — a single running note for fast brain dumps throughout the day

### What NOT to Capture

- Entire articles or books — only the resonant excerpts
- Information you can easily Google
- Content that doesn't pass any of the 4 capture criteria
- Things that make you feel obligated rather than genuinely interested

## Step 2: Organize — Save for Actionability

Once captured, organize information using the PARA method. The biggest mistake is organizing by broad academic subjects, which makes information impossible to find when needed.

### The PARA Method (Summary)

Sort into four categories by actionability:
1. **Projects** — active, short-term, has goal + deadline
2. **Areas** — ongoing, long-term responsibilities
3. **Resources** — topics of interest, future reference
4. **Archives** — inactive items from the other three

### The Organizing Question

When placing a new note, start at the most actionable level:
1. In which **Project** will this be most useful?
2. If none: In which **Area** will this be most useful?
3. If none: Which **Resource** does this belong to?
4. If none: **Archive** it or skip it.

See `para_method.md` for the complete PARA reference.

## Step 3: Distill — Find the Essence

Design notes for your "future self." Because time is scarce, you cannot afford to reread pages of dense notes mid-workday. Boil notes down to their core message so their value can be grasped in seconds.

### Progressive Summarization

Highlight notes in multiple stages ("layers") over time. Each layer adds value to the note, making it progressively easier to scan.

**Layer 1: Original Excerpts**
The raw text you saved — quotes, highlights, passages from source material.

**Layer 2: Bold the Main Points**
Read through the excerpts and **bold** the key sentences, phrases, and keywords that capture the core ideas.

**Layer 3: Highlight the Best of the Bold**
Look *only* at the bolded text and highlight (e.g., with yellow) the absolute best, most insightful sentences. This creates a ultra-scannable summary.

**Layer 4: Executive Summary**
For exceptionally valuable notes only. Write a brief summary in your own words at the very top using bullet points. This is your "TL;DR."

### Key Distillation Principles

- **Distill opportunistically** — do NOT try to perfectly distill every note when you capture it. Wait until you are actually reviewing a note for a specific project.
- **Add value a little at a time** — each time you touch a note, add one more layer.
- **Design for your future self** — assume you will have forgotten all context. Make notes self-explanatory.
- **Favor your own words** — especially at Layer 4. Your own phrasing is faster to process than someone else's prose.

## Step 4: Express — Show Your Work

The ultimate purpose of capturing, organizing, and distilling is to create tangible results. Information only becomes truly valuable knowledge when you put it to use.

### Express Techniques

See `creative_techniques.md` for full details on:
- **Intermediate Packets** — break work into reusable building blocks
- **Archipelago of Ideas** — gather stepping-stone ideas before facing the blank page
- **Hemingway Bridge** — end sessions knowing what comes next
- **Dial Down the Scope** — ship smaller rather than delaying

### The Express Mindset

- Sharing your work is not bragging — it's contributing value
- A finished, imperfect version beats an unfinished, perfect one
- Every output becomes a new Intermediate Packet for future work
- Express early and often — don't wait until everything is "ready"

## AI-Enhanced CODE Workflows

AI tools can accelerate every stage of the CODE framework. Use these patterns with
Claude Code, ChatGPT, Gemini, Notion AI, or any AI assistant.

### AI + Capture

**Processing raw captures:**
Paste messy inputs (meeting transcripts, voice memos, brain dumps, article highlights)
and ask the AI to:
- Extract key insights as bullet points
- Identify action items and tag them with relevant projects
- Format as a structured note with headings, ready for your Second Brain
- Flag which of your 12 Favorite Problems the content relates to

**Example prompt:**
> "Here's a transcript from today's meeting. Extract: (1) decisions made, (2) action
> items with owners, (3) key insights worth capturing in my Second Brain, (4) which
> of my 12 Favorite Problems this relates to: [list your problems]"

**Automated capture pipelines:**
- Readwise → Obsidian/Notion (syncs highlights from Kindle, articles, podcasts)
- Zapier/Make → notes app (auto-captures from RSS, email, social bookmarks)
- Voice memo → Whisper transcription → AI cleanup → notes inbox

### AI + Organize

**Batch PARA classification:**
Feed a collection of unsorted notes to the AI along with your current project list
and areas of responsibility. Ask it to classify each note:

> "Here are my active projects: [list]. Here are my areas: [list]. For each of
> these 15 unsorted notes, tell me: which PARA category does it belong in, and
> specifically which project/area/resource folder?"

**Auto-linking and connection discovery:**
- Obsidian plugins (Smart Connections, Copilot) suggest related notes
- Notion AI finds connections across your workspace
- Ask any AI: "Here are 5 notes on different topics. What connections do you see
  between them that I might have missed?"

### AI + Distill

**AI-assisted Progressive Summarization:**
For long notes or articles, ask the AI to apply the layers:

> "Apply Progressive Summarization to this note:
> 1. Bold the key sentences and phrases (Layer 2)
> 2. From the bolded text, highlight the absolute best insights (Layer 3)
> 3. Write a 2-3 sentence executive summary at the top (Layer 4)
> Keep the original text intact — just add the formatting layers."

**Synthesis across multiple notes:**
> "Here are 8 notes on [topic] from different sources. Synthesize them into one
> distilled note that captures the key insights, areas of agreement, contradictions,
> and gaps."

### AI + Express

**Archipelago of Ideas assembly:**
> "I'm writing [article/report/presentation] about [topic]. Here are my
> Intermediate Packets — notes, quotes, data points, and outlines. Arrange them
> into a logical sequence and draft connecting prose between each section."

**Dial Down the Scope with AI:**
> "I'm overwhelmed by this project. Here's everything I planned to include: [list].
> Help me identify the minimal viable version I could ship this week. What can be
> cut and saved as Intermediate Packets for a future version?"

**Hemingway Bridge generation:**
> "I'm wrapping up a work session on [project]. Here's what I did today: [summary].
> Write a Hemingway Bridge note capturing: current status, next steps, open
> questions, and what I should do first when I return to this."

### AI + Reviews

**Weekly review facilitation:**
> "Walk me through my weekly review. Ask me one question at a time. Start with
> email/inbox clearing, then calendar, then projects, then tasks for next week."

**Project list audit:**
> "Here are my current projects: [list]. For each one, ask me: Does it have a
> clear goal? Does it have a deadline? Have I made progress this month? Help me
> identify false projects, mega-projects, and anything that should be archived."

### Cautions About AI in Your Second Brain

- **AI summaries can lose nuance** — always spot-check, especially for notes you'll
  rely on for important decisions.
- **Don't skip the distillation process entirely.** The act of reading and summarizing
  is itself valuable for learning and retention. Use AI to speed up, not replace,
  your own thinking.
- **Privacy matters.** If your notes contain sensitive personal or business information,
  consider local AI tools (Ollama + Obsidian) over cloud services.
- **AI is a thinking partner, not an oracle.** Use it to find patterns, generate
  alternatives, and challenge assumptions — not to make decisions for you.
