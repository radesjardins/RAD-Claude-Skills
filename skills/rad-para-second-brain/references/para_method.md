# The PARA Method — Complete Reference

## Overview

PARA is a universal system for organizing digital information across any platform. Developed by Tiago Forte, it organizes information by **actionability** rather than by broad subjects. Instead of creating complex hierarchies based on topics like "Psychology" or "Business," you organize files based on how and when you will actually use them.

PARA stands for **Projects, Areas, Resources, and Archives** — four categories that encompass all the information in your life.

## The Four Categories

### 1. Projects — What You Are Working On Right Now

Short-term efforts in your work or personal life that you are actively committed to.

**Rules:**
- Must have a **clear goal or outcome** you are trying to reach
- Must have a **deadline or timeframe** for completion
- Highly actionable and short-term in focus

**Examples:**
- Complete web-page design
- Finish Spanish language course
- Plan summer vacation
- Publish blog post
- Set up new computer
- Write research report

**Not a Project (common mistakes):**
- "Get healthy" (no deadline — this is an Area)
- "Learn photography" (no specific outcome — this is an Area or Resource)
- "Someday write a novel" (dream without a deadline — this is a someday/maybe item)

### 2. Areas of Responsibility — What You Are Committed to Over Time

Ongoing roles or responsibilities that you must manage over time.

**Rules:**
- Never really end — no final objective or deadline
- Have a **standard** that you want to uphold and maintain
- Require ongoing attention
- Slightly less immediately actionable than active projects

**Personal Examples:** Health, Finances, Apartment, Spouse, Car, Cooking, Pets

**Professional Examples:** Direct Reports, Product Development, Human Resources, Marketing, Recruiting, Quality Assurance

**Key Distinction from Projects:**
- Project: "Run a marathon" (specific goal, specific date)
- Area: "Health" (ongoing standard to maintain)
- Projects often *emerge from* Areas when a standard isn't being met

### 3. Resources — Things You Want to Reference in the Future

A personal library or catchall for topics, interests, and useful references that don't belong to an active Project or Area.

**Rules:**
- Currently inactive and low on actionability
- Gathered because of curiosity or potential future usefulness
- No immediate plans to act on it

**Examples:** Habit formation notes, interior design inspiration, stock photos, coffee brewing techniques, saved recipes, architecture, book summaries, typography references

### 4. Archives — Things You Have Completed or Put On Hold

Inactive items from the other three categories.

**Rules:**
- "Cold storage" for your digital life
- Moved here when: a project is completed/canceled, an area of responsibility ends, or a resource is no longer relevant
- Keeps active workspace clear of clutter
- Preserved for easy search and retrieval later

## The Three-Question Sorting Test

When deciding where new information goes, evaluate strictly by actionability (most to least):

1. In which **Project** will this be most useful?
2. If none: In which **Area** will this be most useful?
3. If none: Which **Resource** does this belong to?
4. If none: Place it in **Archives** (or don't save it at all).

## The 10-to-15 Project Rule

Maintain between 10 and 15 active projects at any given time.

- **More than 15:** Attention too fragmented. Unlikely to make visible progress. Leads to discouragement.
- **Fewer than 10:** Risk of stalling. Creative blocks and delays are inevitable — you need other projects to switch to.

**If over 15, identify:**
- **False projects:** Dreams without deadlines or hobbies without goals. Move to Areas, Resources, or someday/maybe.
- **Mega-projects:** Massive efforts that need to be broken into smaller, achievable phases.

## How Items Move Between Categories

PARA is a dynamic production system, not a static filing cabinet. Information flows between categories as life and priorities change. You never have to find the "perfect" permanent place for a file.

**Example flow:**
1. A note on management techniques starts in a **Project** folder: "Management Training Class"
2. After the class, you become a manager — move it to an **Area**: "Direct Reports"
3. You leave the company but stay interested — move to **Resources**: "Coaching"
4. You lose interest entirely — send to **Archives**
5. Years later, you start an executive coaching business — pull from Archives into a new **Project**

## Setting Up PARA — The Clean Slate Approach

**Do NOT** try to sort all your old files into the new folders. This is exhausting and leads to giving up.

**Instead:**
1. Move ALL existing files into a single folder titled "Archive [today's date]"
2. This instantly gives you a clean workspace
3. Pull old files out of the archive via search only when you actually need them
4. Build new PARA folders organically as new projects and needs emerge

## PARA Across Platforms

Mirror the same four top-level folders across every digital tool:
- Notes app (OneNote, Notion, Obsidian, etc.)
- File system (Documents folder, cloud drives)
- Task manager
- Email folders (optional)

This creates consistency so you always know where to find and file things regardless of the platform.
