# Brainstorming Methodology Catalog

This is the master reference for all brainstorming techniques available in the rad-brainstormer plugin. Each technique includes its origin, optimal use case, step-by-step process, an example application, and an honest assessment of strengths and limitations.

---

## Divergent Techniques (Idea Generation)

### 1. SCAMPER

**Origin:** Bob Eberle, 1971. Based on Alex Osborn's brainstorming question lists.

**When to use:** When you have an existing product, process, or concept and want to systematically explore modifications. Excellent for incremental innovation and feature ideation.

**Step-by-step process:**

Work through each letter, asking the associated prompting questions about the subject:

**S — Substitute**
- What materials, components, or people could be substituted?
- What other process could be used?
- What if I changed the rules?
- What other place, time, or approach?
- What if I substitute one part for another?

**C — Combine**
- What ideas, purposes, or functions could be combined?
- What if I combined this with another product or service?
- What materials could be blended?
- What if I combined different talents or resources?
- Can I merge two or more components?

**A — Adapt**
- What else is like this? What other idea does this suggest?
- Does the past offer any parallels?
- What could I copy or borrow from another domain?
- What processes could be adapted?
- What if I changed the context?

**M — Modify / Magnify / Minimize**
- What can be magnified, made larger, or exaggerated?
- What can be made smaller, lighter, or streamlined?
- Can the shape, color, sound, or texture be changed?
- What if I changed the meaning, frequency, or speed?
- Can I add extra features or value?

**P — Put to other use**
- What else could this be used for?
- Are there new ways to use it as-is?
- Could it be used by different people or markets?
- What if it were used in a different context?
- Could waste or by-products be repurposed?

**E — Eliminate**
- What can be removed without loss of function?
- What's non-essential?
- Can I reduce time, effort, or components?
- What happens if I simplify radically?
- What rules or assumptions can I eliminate?

**R — Reverse / Rearrange**
- What if I did the opposite?
- Can I interchange components or steps?
- Can I transpose cause and effect?
- What if I reversed the sequence?
- Can I turn it inside out or upside down?

**Example application:** Improving a mobile banking app. Substitute: replace password login with biometric. Combine: merge savings and investment into one view. Adapt: borrow the "stories" concept from social media for financial tips. Modify: make the transaction history visual with charts. Put to other use: use the app for peer-to-peer lending. Eliminate: remove paper statements entirely. Reverse: let users set their own interest rates for savings goals.

**Strengths:** Highly structured, easy to follow, guarantees breadth of exploration, works for solo or group use.

**Limitations:** Can feel mechanical. Best for iterating on existing things rather than generating entirely new concepts. May produce incremental rather than breakthrough ideas.

### 2. Six Thinking Hats

**Origin:** Edward de Bono, 1985. Published in "Six Thinking Hats."

**When to use:** When a group or individual keeps arguing in circles, mixing facts with feelings with ideas. Forces structured parallel thinking — everyone explores the same dimension at the same time, preventing adversarial debate.

**Step-by-step process:**

Each "hat" represents a mode of thinking. The key rule: everyone wears the same hat at the same time.

**Blue Hat — Process Control**
- Defines the focus and agenda
- Decides which hat to wear next
- Summarizes conclusions
- Use at the beginning and end of the session

**White Hat — Facts and Information**
- What data do we have?
- What data do we need?
- What are the known facts? What are the gaps?
- No opinions or interpretations — just information

**Yellow Hat — Optimism and Benefits**
- What are the benefits?
- What's the best-case scenario?
- What value does this idea create?
- Why might this work?

**Red Hat — Emotions and Intuition**
- What's your gut feeling about this?
- What do you like or dislike instinctively?
- No need to justify — pure feeling
- Quick pulse check (30 seconds per person)

**Green Hat — Creativity and New Ideas**
- Generate alternatives and new possibilities
- "What if..." thinking
- No evaluation during green hat
- Build on others' ideas

**Black Hat — Caution and Critical Judgment**
- What could go wrong?
- What are the risks and weaknesses?
- Does this fit our constraints?
- Where are the logical flaws?

**Recommended hat sequence for exploring a new idea:**
1. Blue (set the focus) - 2 minutes
2. White (gather facts) - 5 minutes
3. Green (generate ideas) - 10 minutes
4. Yellow (explore benefits) - 5 minutes
5. Black (identify risks) - 5 minutes
6. Red (gut check) - 2 minutes
7. Blue (summarize and decide next steps) - 3 minutes

**Recommended hat sequence for evaluating an existing proposal:**
1. Blue (set the focus) - 2 minutes
2. White (state the facts) - 5 minutes
3. Yellow (state the benefits) - 5 minutes
4. Black (state the risks) - 5 minutes
5. Green (address the risks creatively) - 5 minutes
6. Red (final gut check) - 2 minutes
7. Blue (decide) - 3 minutes

**Example application:** Evaluating whether to pivot a SaaS product to target enterprise customers. White: current revenue is $50K MRR from SMBs, enterprise deals average 10x larger. Yellow: massive revenue upside, stronger retention. Black: longer sales cycles, need compliance features, may lose SMB DNA. Green: what if we created an enterprise tier while keeping SMB? Red: team feels excited but scared. Blue: decision to pilot with 3 enterprise prospects before committing.

**Strengths:** Prevents endless debate, ensures all perspectives are heard, separates emotion from logic, works beautifully for group decision-making.

**Limitations:** Can feel artificial if the group isn't bought in. Requires a facilitator (Blue Hat) to keep discipline. Less useful for pure idea generation (use Green Hat portion, but there are better tools for that).

### 3. Reverse Brainstorming

**Origin:** Evolved from various creative problem-solving traditions. Sometimes called "Negative Brainstorming."

**When to use:** When the group is stuck and can't generate positive ideas. When you want to uncover hidden risks or failure modes. Great icebreaker because terrible ideas are funny and lower inhibition.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Clearly state the problem you're trying to solve
2. Reverse it: "How could we make this problem WORSE?" or "How could we guarantee failure?"
3. Generate as many ways to make it worse as possible (this is usually easy and fun)
4. For each "worse" idea, reverse it back: "What's the opposite of this? What would prevent this?"
5. Evaluate the reversed ideas — many will be genuinely useful insights

**Example application:** Problem: "How do we improve customer onboarding?" Reversed: "How do we make onboarding as terrible as possible?" Ideas: send no welcome email, make the UI confusing, require 20 form fields, provide no help documentation, make them wait 3 days for account activation. Reversed back: send a warm welcome sequence, simplify the UI to 3 steps, ask only essential info, provide contextual tooltips, activate instantly.

**Strengths:** Breaks through creative blocks, produces laughter (lowers tension), reveals hidden assumptions about failure modes, easy to facilitate.

**Limitations:** Ideas tend to be "fix what's broken" rather than truly novel. Works better for process improvement than blue-sky innovation. Can become cynical if not redirected.

### 4. Starbursting

**Origin:** Developed as an alternative to traditional brainstorming, focusing on questions rather than answers.

**When to use:** Early-stage problem exploration when the problem itself isn't well-defined. Before jumping to solutions. When you suspect the team is solving the wrong problem.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Place the idea, concept, or problem at the center of a 6-pointed star
2. Each point represents one question word: Who, What, When, Where, Why, How
3. Generate as many questions as possible for each point
4. Do NOT answer the questions yet — just generate them
5. Prioritize the most important unanswered questions
6. Use the questions to guide research and further brainstorming

**Question prompts per point:**
- **Who:** Who is affected? Who would use this? Who decides? Who benefits? Who loses? Who should be involved?
- **What:** What is the core problem? What are we assuming? What's been tried? What would success look like? What are the constraints?
- **When:** When does this problem occur? When is the deadline? When would people use this? When did this start?
- **Where:** Where does this happen? Where else has this been solved? Where should we look for inspiration?
- **Why:** Why is this a problem now? Why hasn't it been solved? Why do people care? Why should we solve it?
- **How:** How big is the problem? How will we measure success? How much can we invest? How will people discover this?

**Example application:** A startup considering a new product. Instead of brainstorming features, starburst first: Who exactly has this problem? (turns out it's a narrower segment than assumed). Why haven't they solved it already? (reveals they have partial solutions). When do they encounter it? (only during quarterly reporting — not daily). This reframes the entire product direction.

**Strengths:** Prevents premature solution-jumping, reveals knowledge gaps, frames the problem properly before ideation, easy to facilitate.

**Limitations:** Doesn't generate solutions — must be followed by a generative technique. Can feel slow if people are eager to ideate. Some questions may feel redundant.

### 5. How Might We (HMW)

**Origin:** Min Basadur at Procter & Gamble in the 1970s. Popularized by IDEO and Google Design Sprints.

**When to use:** Transitioning from problem understanding to solution generation. Reframing observations and pain points into actionable opportunity statements.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Start with a problem statement, user need, or observation
2. Reframe as "How might we [desired outcome]?"
3. Calibrate scope (see guidance below)
4. Use the HMW as a launch pad for ideation

**Scope calibration guidance:**

Too broad: "How might we fix healthcare?" (Impossible to act on.)
Too narrow: "How might we add a blue button to the settings page?" (Already a solution, not an opportunity.)
Just right: "How might we help patients track their medication without adding burden to their daily routine?"

**Scope tests:**
- Does it make you think of multiple possible solutions? (Good — it's at the right level)
- Does it have only one obvious answer? (Too narrow — zoom out)
- Does it feel overwhelming with no clear starting point? (Too broad — zoom in)
- Does it contain a solution? Remove the solution and restate the need

**Formula variations:**
- "How might we [verb] [user] [outcome]?"
- "How might we [remove barrier] so that [user] can [goal]?"
- "How might we [improve aspect] without [tradeoff]?"

**Example application:** Observation: "Parents forget to give kids their vitamins." Bad HMW: "How might we remind parents?" (too narrow, already implies notification). Good HMW: "How might we make children's vitamin routines effortless for busy parents?" This opens up solutions like: gummy vitamins that kids ask for, vitamin-infused breakfast foods, smart dispensers, school-based programs, etc.

**Strengths:** Simple and powerful, reframes problems as opportunities, calibrates scope naturally, universally understood format.

**Limitations:** Requires a well-understood problem first (pair with Starbursting). Can become a crutch phrase that replaces actual thinking. Quality depends entirely on scope calibration.

### 6. TRIZ

**Origin:** Genrich Altshuller, USSR, 1946 onward. Analyzed 200,000+ patents to identify 40 inventive principles. TRIZ stands for "Theory of Inventive Problem Solving" (in Russian).

**When to use:** Technical or engineering problems. When the problem involves a contradiction ("I need X but it conflicts with Y"). When you want solutions grounded in proven innovation patterns rather than random ideation.

**The Contradiction Matrix concept:**
TRIZ is built on the insight that most inventive problems involve a contradiction: improving one parameter worsens another. The contradiction matrix maps 39 engineering parameters against each other and suggests which of the 40 principles typically resolve each type of contradiction.

**The 10 most commonly applicable inventive principles:**

**Principle 1: Segmentation**
- Divide an object or system into independent parts
- Make it sectional or modular
- Increase the degree of fragmentation
- Example: Modular furniture, microservices architecture, playlist segmentation

**Principle 2: Taking Out / Extraction**
- Separate an interfering part or property
- Extract only the necessary part or property
- Example: Noise-canceling headphones (extract the noise), separating data from presentation (MVC pattern)

**Principle 5: Merging / Consolidation**
- Combine identical or similar objects or operations
- Combine operations in time (do them simultaneously)
- Example: Swiss Army knife, all-in-one dashboards, bundled services

**Principle 10: Preliminary Action**
- Perform required changes in advance (fully or partially)
- Pre-arrange objects for the most convenient position
- Example: Pre-configured templates, pre-loaded caches, mise en place in cooking

**Principle 13: The Other Way Around / Inversion**
- Instead of doing what the problem suggests, do the opposite
- Make a moving part fixed, or a fixed part moving
- Turn the process upside down
- Example: Instead of bringing customers to the store, bring the store to customers (mobile retail)

**Principle 15: Dynamization**
- Allow an object or process to change to be optimal at each stage
- Divide into parts that can move relative to each other
- If fixed, make it movable or adaptable
- Example: Responsive web design, dynamic pricing, adjustable standing desks

**Principle 18: Mechanical Vibration / Pulsation**
- Use oscillation instead of continuous action
- If already oscillating, increase frequency
- Use resonance
- Example: Pulse surveys instead of annual reviews, sprint cycles instead of continuous development

**Principle 25: Self-Service**
- Make the object serve itself and carry out supplementary functions
- Use waste resources or energy
- Example: Self-checkout, user-generated content, auto-scaling infrastructure

**Principle 28: Mechanics Substitution**
- Replace a mechanical system with a sensory one (optical, acoustic, taste, smell)
- Use fields (electric, magnetic, electromagnetic)
- Example: Replace physical buttons with touchscreen, replace physical mail with email

**Principle 35: Parameter Change**
- Change an object's physical state, concentration, density, flexibility, or temperature
- Change other parameters
- Example: Cloud vs on-premise (change where computation happens), subscription vs purchase (change the payment model)

**Step-by-step process (simplified for brainstorming):**
1. Define the problem clearly
2. Identify the contradiction: "I want to improve [parameter A] but it worsens [parameter B]"
3. Review the 10 principles above (or all 40 for deep work)
4. For each potentially relevant principle, ask: "How could this principle resolve my contradiction?"
5. Generate specific solution concepts from the abstract principles
6. Evaluate the concepts

**Example application:** Problem: "Our app needs more features (value) but must remain simple (usability)." Contradiction: feature richness vs simplicity. Applicable principles: Segmentation (progressive disclosure — show features in layers), Dynamization (adaptive UI that shows relevant features based on user behavior), Taking Out (separate advanced features into a "pro" mode).

**Strengths:** Grounded in proven patterns across millions of inventions, systematic rather than random, excellent for technical problems with clear contradictions.

**Limitations:** Steep learning curve, feels abstract until practiced, most powerful for engineering/technical domains, requires identifying the contradiction clearly.

### 7. Morphological Analysis

**Origin:** Fritz Zwicky, 1940s. Originally used in astrophysics and jet propulsion engineering.

**When to use:** When you want to systematically explore the entire solution space. When a problem can be decomposed into independent dimensions. Excellent for product configuration and business model design.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Define the problem or system to be designed
2. Identify the key dimensions (parameters) — typically 3-7
3. For each dimension, list all possible options (at least 3-5 per dimension)
4. Create a matrix with dimensions as rows and options as columns
5. Select one option from each dimension and combine them into a complete concept
6. Explore combinations systematically or use random selection for surprise

**Example application:** Designing a new coffee shop concept.

| Dimension | Option A | Option B | Option C | Option D |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Location | Downtown | Suburban | Mobile truck | Inside bookstore |
| Service model | Counter | Table service | Self-service | Subscription |
| Menu focus | Single-origin | Blended drinks | Food-forward | Health drinks |
| Atmosphere | Minimalist | Cozy/rustic | High-tech | Outdoor garden |
| Price point | Premium | Mid-range | Budget | Pay-what-you-want |

Combination 1: Mobile truck + Subscription + Single-origin + Minimalist + Premium = A premium mobile espresso subscription that visits offices.

Combination 2: Inside bookstore + Self-service + Food-forward + Cozy/rustic + Mid-range = A self-serve cafe inside a bookstore with a serious food menu.

The total solution space here is 4 x 4 x 4 x 4 x 4 = 1,024 possible concepts.

**Strengths:** Ensures exhaustive exploration, reveals unexpected combinations, highly visual and structured, prevents fixation on obvious solutions.

**Limitations:** Dimension selection is critical (garbage in, garbage out). Combinatorial explosion with too many dimensions. Many combinations will be nonsensical — filtering is needed.

### 8. Crazy 8s

**Origin:** Google Ventures Design Sprint methodology (Jake Knapp, 2010s).

**When to use:** Warm-up exercise before deeper brainstorming. When you need to break through initial hesitation. When quantity matters more than quality. Early in a session to generate raw material.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Fold a sheet of paper into 8 sections (or prepare 8 numbered slots)
2. Set a timer for 8 minutes
3. Sketch or write one idea per section — one minute each
4. No erasing, no self-editing, no going back
5. When time's up, review all 8 ideas
6. Star the most promising ones
7. Share and discuss

**Adapted for AI sessions:**
1. Ask the user to rapid-fire 8 ideas in quick succession
2. If they slow down, prompt: "Don't overthink — what's the first thing that comes to mind?"
3. After all 8, review together and identify themes or favorites
4. Alternatively, both user and AI generate 4 each, then combine and discuss

**Example application:** "We need a new feature for our project management tool. Go — 8 ideas, rapid fire." 1) AI standup summaries 2) Mood tracker per task 3) Auto-deadlines based on similar past projects 4) Voice note attachments 5) Random coffee chat scheduling 6) Dependency visualizer 7) "Focus mode" that hides everything except current task 8) Client portal for status updates.

**Strengths:** Fast, lowers the bar for participation, forces quantity, great warm-up, prevents overthinking.

**Limitations:** Ideas are shallow by design — need follow-up exploration. Some people freeze under time pressure. Quality varies enormously. Best as a starting point, not an end point.

### 9. 5 Whys

**Origin:** Sakichi Toyoda, Toyota Production System, 1930s. Widely used in lean manufacturing and root cause analysis.

**When to use:** When you suspect the stated problem isn't the real problem. Before brainstorming solutions, to ensure you're solving the right thing. During problem definition phase.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. State the problem
2. Ask "Why does this happen?" — write the answer
3. Ask "Why?" again about that answer
4. Repeat until you reach a root cause (typically 3-7 levels, not always exactly 5)
5. The root cause often reveals the real problem to solve

**Key facilitation notes:**
- Accept multiple branches at each level — problems often have multiple causes
- Don't accept vague answers ("because it's always been that way") — probe deeper
- Stop when you reach something actionable or a fundamental constraint
- The goal is insight, not blame

**Example application:**
- Problem: "Users aren't completing our onboarding flow."
- Why? "They drop off at step 3 (connecting their data source)."
- Why? "The integration setup is confusing."
- Why? "We require them to configure 12 settings manually."
- Why? "Our API needs all 12 parameters to function."
- Why? "We never built sensible defaults."
- Root cause insight: The real problem isn't UX design — it's engineering defaults. The brainstorming session should focus on "How do we create smart defaults for integration setup?" rather than "How do we make the onboarding prettier?"

**Strengths:** Simple, fast, reveals root causes, prevents solving symptoms, easy to facilitate.

**Limitations:** Can oversimplify complex problems. May lead down one causal chain while missing others. Depends on the quality of answers at each level. Not an ideation technique — it's a problem definition technique.

### 10. Random Entry / Lateral Thinking

**Origin:** Edward de Bono, 1960s-70s. Part of the broader "Lateral Thinking" toolkit.

**When to use:** When conventional approaches aren't producing novel ideas. When you need to break out of familiar thought patterns. When the group is anchored on one solution direction.

**Step-by-step process:**

**Random Word technique:**
1. Get a random word (flip to a random page in a dictionary, use a random word generator, or pick any noun)
2. List the attributes, associations, and functions of that random word
3. Force connections between those attributes and your problem
4. Develop any promising connections into actual ideas

**Provocations (PO):**
1. Make a deliberately provocative statement about the problem (prefixed with "PO" to signal it's not a real proposal)
2. Example: "PO: What if customers paid us NOT to use our product?"
3. Use the provocation as a stepping stone — what does it make you think of?
4. Extract the useful movement from the provocation

**Challenge:**
1. Take something everyone assumes is true or necessary
2. Ask: "Why do we do it this way? Does it have to be this way?"
3. If the answer is "because we've always done it this way" — you've found an opportunity

**Concept Extraction:**
1. Look at a successful solution in any domain
2. Extract the underlying concept (not the specific implementation)
3. Apply that concept to your problem
4. Example: Uber's concept isn't "car rides" — it's "matching idle resources with demand in real time." Where else could that concept apply?

**Example application:** Problem: improving employee engagement. Random word: "lighthouse." Attributes: tall, visible, guides ships, warning, light, rotating, coastal. Forced connections: What if we made recognition "visible from far away" (public dashboards)? What if mentors were "lighthouses" — stationed at key positions to guide newcomers? What if we created "warning systems" for burnout? The "rotating" idea leads to rotating team leads.

**Strengths:** Breaks fixation patterns, produces genuinely novel ideas, fun and surprising, works when other techniques feel stale.

**Limitations:** Requires comfort with ambiguity. Connections can feel forced. Hit rate is low — most connections won't work, but the ones that do are often breakthrough ideas. Not suitable for people who need structure.

### 11. Brainwriting 6-3-5

**Origin:** Bernd Rohrbach, 1968. Published in the German sales magazine "Absatzwirtschaft."

**When to use:** When vocal dominance is a concern in groups. When you want to build systematically on others' ideas. Alternative to verbal brainstorming for introverts.

**Original process (group of 6):**
1. 6 participants each write 3 ideas on a sheet of paper (5 minutes)
2. Pass sheets to the right
3. Read the 3 ideas on the sheet you received
4. Add 3 new ideas, inspired by or building on the existing ones (5 minutes)
5. Repeat for 5 rounds (6 rounds total including the original)
6. Result: 108 ideas in 30 minutes (6 people x 3 ideas x 6 rounds)

**Adapted for 1-on-1 AI sessions:**
1. User writes 3 initial ideas
2. AI builds on those 3 ideas, adding 3 variations or extensions
3. User responds to AI's additions with 3 more ideas
4. AI builds again
5. Continue for 4-5 rounds
6. Review all ideas together

**Key rule:** Each round must build on or be inspired by the previous round's ideas. No repeating. The goal is evolution, not independent generation.

**Example application:** Topic: ways to reduce food waste in restaurants.
Round 1 (user): 1) Smaller portion options, 2) Donate leftovers to food banks, 3) Compost program.
Round 2 (AI builds): 1) Dynamic portion sizing based on time of day, 2) Partner with apps like Too Good To Go for end-of-day surplus, 3) On-site composting that produces soil for a rooftop herb garden.
Round 3 (user): 1) AI-predicted demand to adjust prep quantities, 2) "Surprise bag" menu item using whatever's about to expire, 3) Customer-facing waste tracker showing the restaurant's environmental impact.

**Strengths:** Produces highly developed ideas through iterative building, eliminates groupthink dynamics, structured yet creative, every idea gets attention.

**Limitations:** Requires ideas to be written clearly enough for others to build on. Can converge too quickly if builders are too similar. The adapted 1-on-1 version loses the diversity of 6 perspectives.

### 12. Mind Mapping

**Origin:** Popularized by Tony Buzan in the 1970s, though the concept of radial diagrams has ancient roots.

**When to use:** When you need to organize a complex topic before brainstorming. When exploring relationships between concepts. Both as a divergent technique (free association) and as an organizational tool.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Write the central topic/problem in the middle
2. Draw main branches for primary themes or categories
3. Add sub-branches for related ideas, details, and connections
4. Use single words or short phrases (not sentences)
5. Connect branches that relate to each other across the map
6. Use visual hierarchy: thick branches for main themes, thinner for details

**Adapted for text-based AI sessions:**
Use indented lists to represent the branching structure:

```
Central Topic
├── Branch 1
│   ├── Sub-idea 1a
│   ├── Sub-idea 1b
│   └── Sub-idea 1c
├── Branch 2
│   ├── Sub-idea 2a
│   └── Sub-idea 2b
└── Branch 3
    ├── Sub-idea 3a
    └── Sub-idea 3b
```

**Example application:** Central topic: "Remote Work Challenges"
- Communication: async vs sync, time zones, tool overload, information silos
- Culture: loneliness, trust, onboarding new hires, celebrating wins
- Productivity: distractions at home, overwork, meeting fatigue, deep work time
- Infrastructure: internet reliability, equipment, security, ergonomics

**Strengths:** Visual and intuitive, reveals connections, good for organizing before or after brainstorming, scalable to any complexity.

**Limitations:** Can become messy and hard to follow if too large. Text-based versions lose the spatial element. Better for organizing than generating truly novel ideas.

### 13. Analogical Thinking

**Origin:** A fundamental cognitive strategy used throughout history. Formalized in design thinking and innovation methodologies.

**When to use:** When you want truly novel solutions by importing ideas from other domains. When the team is stuck in industry-conventional thinking. When facing a problem that has likely been solved elsewhere.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Define the core challenge in abstract terms (strip away domain-specific language)
2. Identify analogous domains: "Who else faces this kind of problem?"
3. Study how those domains solve it
4. Extract the underlying principle
5. Apply the principle to your specific context
6. Adapt and refine

**Analogy prompts:**
- "How would [Apple/Amazon/Netflix/IKEA] approach this?"
- "How does nature solve this?" (Biomimicry)
- "What would a [doctor/chef/architect/teacher] do?"
- "How do [sports teams/orchestras/emergency rooms] handle this?"
- "What industry has already perfected this?"

**Example application:** Problem: reducing customer churn in a SaaS product. Analogous domain: fitness/gym industry (also struggles with commitment and churn). What gyms do: personal trainers (onboarding specialists), group classes (community features), progress tracking (usage dashboards), habit stacking (integrate into routine). Applied: assign each new user a success coach for the first 30 days, create user cohort groups, build an impact dashboard showing value delivered, integrate with tools they already use daily.

**Strengths:** Produces genuinely novel cross-pollinated ideas, breaks industry tunnel vision, builds on proven solutions from other domains.

**Limitations:** Analogies can be misleading if the domains are too different. Requires broad knowledge to identify good analogues. Surface-level analogies ("be the Uber of X") are cliches — dig deeper.

### 14. Worst Possible Idea

**Origin:** A variation of Reverse Brainstorming, formalized in various design thinking curricula.

**When to use:** As a warm-up or icebreaker. When the group is too cautious or self-censoring. When perfectionism is blocking ideation. When energy is low.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. State the problem clearly
2. Ask: "What is the absolute WORST solution you can think of?"
3. Encourage truly terrible ideas — the worse, the better
4. Generate many bad ideas quickly (laughter is expected and welcome)
5. For each bad idea, ask: "What makes this bad?" and "Is there a kernel of something useful here?"
6. Flip the bad ideas: "What if the opposite of this terrible idea were actually good?"
7. Some "worst" ideas contain hidden innovation — things that are bad by convention but might work in a new context

**Example application:** Problem: "How do we get more user feedback?" Worst ideas: Spam users with surveys every 5 minutes. Pay users $1,000 per survey. Force them to write feedback before accessing features. Make the CEO personally call every user. Only accept feedback via fax. Hidden gems: What if we asked one micro-question at natural breakpoints (not every 5 minutes, but at the right moments)? What if we offered meaningful incentives (not $1,000, but early access to features)? What if the CEO actually did send personal video messages to power users?

**Strengths:** Lowers the bar for participation, generates laughter and energy, paradoxically produces usable ideas, frees people from perfectionism.

**Limitations:** Can devolve into pure silliness without productive output. Some participants find it hard to be deliberately bad. Must be followed by serious evaluation.

## Convergent Techniques (Idea Evaluation)

### 15. Impact/Effort Matrix

**Origin:** Common in project management and lean methodologies. Also known as the 2x2 Priority Matrix.

**When to use:** When you have many ideas and need to prioritize quickly. When resources are limited and you need to choose what to pursue first.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. List all ideas to be evaluated
2. For each idea, rate Impact (1-5) considering: business value, user value, strategic alignment, competitive advantage
3. For each idea, rate Effort (1-5) considering: time required, technical complexity, dependencies, risk level
4. Plot on a 2x2 grid:
   - **Quick Wins** (high impact, low effort) — Do these first
   - **Major Projects** (high impact, high effort) — Plan and resource these
   - **Fill-ins** (low impact, low effort) — Do when you have spare capacity
   - **Hard Slogs** (low impact, high effort) — Avoid or reconsider

**Template for presenting results:**

```
         HIGH IMPACT
              |
  Major       |      Quick
  Projects    |      Wins ★
              |
HIGH EFFORT --+-- LOW EFFORT
              |
  Hard        |      Fill-ins
  Slogs ✗     |
              |
         LOW IMPACT
```

**Strengths:** Fast, intuitive, visual, creates clear priorities, widely understood.

**Limitations:** Ratings are subjective. "Impact" and "effort" can mean different things to different people — define them clearly. Doesn't account for dependencies between ideas.

### 16. Assumption Mapping

**Origin:** David Bland, Strategyzer. Central to the "Testing Business Ideas" methodology.

**When to use:** After selecting an idea but before committing resources. When you need to identify what could kill the idea and what to validate first.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Ask: "What needs to be true for this idea to work?"
2. List all assumptions across four categories:
   - **Desirability:** Will people want this? (user need, willingness to switch, perceived value)
   - **Feasibility:** Can we build this? (technical capability, skills, partnerships)
   - **Viability:** Should we build this? (revenue model, cost structure, market size)
   - **Adaptability:** Can we sustain this? (regulatory changes, competitive response, scalability)
3. Map each assumption on a 2x2 matrix:
   - X-axis: Importance (how critical is this assumption?)
   - Y-axis: Evidence (how much evidence do we have?)
4. Focus on the "Leap of Faith" quadrant: high importance, low evidence
5. Design the cheapest, fastest experiment to test each risky assumption

**Example application:** Idea: subscription-based meal planning app. Assumptions mapped:
- High importance, low evidence (test first): "Busy parents will pay $15/month for meal planning" — test with landing page and pre-orders
- High importance, high evidence (monitor): "People use phones to plan meals" — validated by existing app usage data
- Low importance, high evidence (ignore): "Users prefer dark mode" — nice to have, not critical
- Low importance, low evidence (skip): "Users want to share meal plans on social media" — unimportant even if true

**Strengths:** Systematic risk identification, focuses scarce resources on the riskiest assumptions, prevents building on unvalidated foundations.

**Limitations:** Requires honest self-assessment about what you don't know. Can be uncomfortable for teams invested in an idea. Importance and evidence are subjective.

### 17. Opportunity Solution Trees

**Origin:** Teresa Torres, published in "Continuous Discovery Habits" (2021).

**When to use:** Product discovery. When you need to connect brainstormed solutions back to outcomes. When you have many ideas and need to determine which serve the most important opportunities.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Define the desired **Outcome** (business or product outcome, measurable)
2. Identify **Opportunities** (user needs, pain points, desires that, if addressed, drive the outcome)
3. For each opportunity, brainstorm **Solutions** (specific ideas that address the opportunity)
4. For each solution, design **Experiments** (the cheapest way to test the solution)

**Key principle:** "Don't brainstorm across your entire tree." Pick one target opportunity and brainstorm solutions for that specific opportunity. This prevents scattered ideation.

**Tree structure:**
```
Desired Outcome
├── Opportunity A
│   ├── Solution A1 → Experiment
│   ├── Solution A2 → Experiment
│   └── Solution A3 → Experiment
├── Opportunity B (TARGET)
│   ├── Solution B1 → Experiment
│   ├── Solution B2 → Experiment
│   └── Solution B3 → Experiment
└── Opportunity C
    └── (brainstorm later)
```

**Example application:** Outcome: Increase weekly active users by 20%. Opportunities: new users don't understand the value (onboarding), existing users forget to come back (retention), users can't do their core task efficiently (usability). Target opportunity: existing users forget to come back. Solutions: smart notification triggers, weekly email digest, integration with daily tools (Slack/Teams), streak/gamification mechanics.

**Strengths:** Connects ideas to outcomes, prevents brainstorming without direction, provides a clear framework for prioritization, iterative.

**Limitations:** Requires a clear desired outcome (not always available early). Can be overly structured for exploratory brainstorming. Best for product teams with defined metrics.

### 18. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)

**Origin:** Clayton Christensen (Harvard Business School) and Tony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation). Popularized in "Competing Against Luck" (2016).

**When to use:** When evaluating whether an idea truly serves user needs. When you want to understand WHY users would adopt a solution. During product discovery and value proposition design.

**The three job dimensions:**

- **Functional job:** The practical task the user is trying to accomplish. "Help me get from A to B quickly."
- **Social job:** How the user wants to be perceived by others. "Make me look professional to my colleagues."
- **Emotional job:** How the user wants to feel. "Give me confidence that I'm making the right decision."

**The Outcome-Driven Innovation process (Ulwick):**
1. Define the job as a job statement: "[When _situation_], I want to [_motivation_], so I can [_expected outcome_]."
2. Create a job map: the steps the user goes through to get the job done
3. For each step, identify desired outcomes: "[Direction] + [measure] + [object of control] + [context]" (e.g., "Minimize the time it takes to identify the right contact")
4. Survey to find underserved outcomes (high importance, low satisfaction)
5. Brainstorm solutions targeting underserved outcomes

**Interview questions to uncover jobs:**
- "Tell me about the last time you [relevant activity]. Walk me through what happened."
- "What were you trying to accomplish?"
- "What alternatives did you consider?"
- "What was frustrating about the process?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand, what would you change?"

**Example application:** People don't buy a quarter-inch drill because they want a quarter-inch drill. They buy it because they want a quarter-inch hole. But actually, they want to hang a shelf. But actually, they want an organized living space. The job-to-be-done is "help me feel organized and in control of my home." This opens solutions far beyond drills: adhesive mounting systems, modular storage, professional organizer services, furniture with built-in storage.

**Strengths:** Deeply user-centered, reveals non-obvious competitors, frames evaluation around real needs rather than feature comparisons.

**Limitations:** Requires user research to do well (hard to guess jobs accurately). Can be time-consuming. Functional jobs are easier to identify than social/emotional ones.

### 19. Dot Voting (Adapted for 1-on-1 AI Sessions)

**Origin:** Traditional facilitation technique, widely used in workshops and design sprints.

**When to use:** When you have 5 or more ideas and need to narrow down. When you want transparent, multi-criteria evaluation.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. List all candidate ideas
2. Define 3-5 evaluation criteria (e.g., impact, feasibility, novelty, user value, strategic fit)
3. Optionally assign weights to criteria (e.g., impact = 2x, novelty = 1x)
4. Score each idea 1-5 on each criterion
5. Calculate weighted total for each idea
6. Rank ideas by total score
7. Discuss: Do the rankings feel right? Any surprises?

**Template:**

| Idea | Impact (2x) | Feasibility (1x) | Novelty (1x) | Alignment (1.5x) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Idea A | 4 (8) | 3 (3) | 5 (5) | 4 (6) | 22 |
| Idea B | 5 (10) | 2 (2) | 3 (3) | 5 (7.5) | 22.5 |
| Idea C | 3 (6) | 5 (5) | 2 (2) | 3 (4.5) | 17.5 |

**Facilitation tip:** Have the user score first, then offer your perspective. Compare and discuss differences. The conversation about WHY scores differ is often more valuable than the scores themselves.

**Strengths:** Transparent, multi-dimensional, creates a shareable artifact, surfaces disagreements constructively.

**Limitations:** Still subjective — just more structured subjectivity. Can give false precision (a score of 22 vs 22.5 is meaningless). Numbers should guide discussion, not replace judgment.

### 20. Pre-Mortem Analysis

**Origin:** Gary Klein, research psychologist, 1989. Published as "Performing a Project Pre-Mortem" in Harvard Business Review.

**When to use:** After selecting a top idea but before committing to it. When you want to identify risks that optimism blindness would otherwise hide. When the team is excited and needs a structured dose of skepticism.

**Step-by-step process:**
1. Assume the idea has been implemented and has failed spectacularly
2. Set the timeframe: "It's 6 months from now and this has completely failed."
3. Each participant independently writes reasons for the failure
4. Share all failure scenarios
5. Categorize failures: preventable vs. unpreventable, likely vs. unlikely
6. For each likely, preventable failure: design a mitigation strategy
7. Decide: proceed (with mitigations), pivot, or abandon

**Adapted for 1-on-1 AI sessions:**
1. AI presents 4-5 failure scenarios from different angles (technical, market, team, user, competitive)
2. User adds their own failure scenarios
3. Together, rate each on likelihood and severity
4. Identify the top 3 risks
5. Brainstorm mitigations for each

**Example application:** Idea: launch a premium tier for a free productivity tool. Pre-mortem failure scenarios: Free users revolt and leave (community backlash). Premium features aren't compelling enough (low conversion). Supporting two tiers doubles engineering complexity. Competitors match our premium features in their free tier. The pricing alienates our core demographic. Mitigations: grandfather existing users, validate willingness-to-pay before building, keep tiers on same codebase with feature flags.

**Strengths:** Legitimizes pessimism, surfaces risks early, cheap to perform, prevents groupthink optimism, based on research showing 30% better risk identification than standard methods.

**Limitations:** Can kill enthusiasm if not facilitated well — frame it as "making the idea stronger" not "finding reasons to quit." May surface risks that are already known. Depends on participants' ability to imagine failure modes.

## Method Selection Guide

Use this table to determine the best technique sequence based on your starting situation.

| Starting State | Recommended Sequence | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| "I have nothing" | Starbursting -> HMW -> Crazy 8s -> Impact/Effort | Explore the problem space first, then frame opportunities, then generate rapidly, then prioritize |
| "I have a vague idea" | SCAMPER -> Reverse Brainstorm -> Analogical Thinking -> Assumption Mapping | Systematically explore the idea, stress-test it, cross-pollinate, then validate assumptions |
| "I have a clear idea, want alternatives" | Six Thinking Hats -> Morphological Analysis -> Dot Voting | Examine the idea from all angles, explore the full solution space, then select the best |
| "Improving something existing" | SCAMPER -> 5 Whys -> TRIZ (if technical) -> Impact/Effort | Modify systematically, find root causes, resolve contradictions, then prioritize changes |
| "Stuck / blocked" | Worst Possible Idea -> Random Entry -> Crazy 8s -> HMW | Lower the bar, break patterns, generate quantity, then reframe as opportunities |
| "Have many ideas, need to filter" | Pre-Mortem -> Assumption Mapping -> Impact/Effort -> JTBD lens | Stress-test, identify riskiest assumptions, prioritize, then check against real user needs |
| "Product discovery" | JTBD -> Opportunity Solution Trees -> HMW -> Assumption Mapping | Start with user jobs, map opportunities, generate targeted solutions, validate assumptions |
| "Technical problem" | 5 Whys -> TRIZ -> Morphological Analysis -> Impact/Effort | Find the root cause, apply inventive principles, explore full solution space, prioritize |
| "Team alignment needed" | Six Thinking Hats -> Dot Voting -> Pre-Mortem -> Impact/Effort | Structured parallel thinking, transparent scoring, shared risk assessment, clear priorities |
| "Creative/content project" | Mind Mapping -> Analogical Thinking -> Crazy 8s -> Dot Voting | Organize the landscape, cross-pollinate, generate rapidly, select the best |
