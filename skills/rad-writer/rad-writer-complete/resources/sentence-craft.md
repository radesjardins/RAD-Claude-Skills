# Sentence Craft Reference

The mechanical craft of writing sentences, paragraphs, and transitions that don't sound like a machine wrote them. These are teachable patterns, not abstract principles.

---

## Burstiness: Sentence Length as a Tool

### The Core Principle

Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences punch. Long sentences build complexity, carry the reader through a layered thought, and create momentum through subordinate clauses and accumulated detail. The contrast between the two creates rhythm.

This is the single most impactful craft technique for avoiding AI-typical prose. AI produces sentences of uniform medium length (15-25 words, SD < 5). Human writing oscillates dramatically (SD > 8).

### Techniques

**The short punch after a long build:**
> The committee spent three months reviewing applications, interviewing candidates, conducting background checks, and debating the merits of twelve finalists who each brought distinct qualifications to a role that had been vacant for nearly a year. They picked Sarah.

**The fragment for emphasis:**
> The proposal covered everything — staffing, budget, timeline, risk mitigation, stakeholder alignment. Everything except the one question the board actually cared about. Why.

**The one-sentence paragraph:**

Use sparingly. When you drop a single sentence into its own paragraph, it carries the weight of everything around it. Overuse dilutes the effect.

**Sentence types for variation:**

| Type | What it does | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Cumulative (loose)** | Main clause first, then adds details. Most natural. | "The fire truck arrived, sirens blaring, lights flashing, neighbors gathering on porches." |
| **Periodic** | Delays main clause to the end. Builds suspense. | "Despite the rain, the cold, and the three-hour delay, she finished the race." |
| **Balanced** | Two halves of equal weight. Best for contrast. | "The optimist sees the doughnut; the pessimist sees the hole." |
| **Inverted** | Reverses normal SVO order for emphasis. | "Gone were the days of easy victories." |
| **Fragment** | Intentionally incomplete. Emphasis and pacing. | "Not a chance." / "Everywhere." / "The worst kind." |

**Length patterns with names:**

- **Long + Short (The Punch):** Build complexity, then drop a short sentence for impact. "That night I sat on Tyan-yu's bed and waited for him to touch me. But he didn't." (Amy Tan)
- **Short + Long (The Hook):** Surprising short statement, then expand. "She lied. The whole story, from the opening line about her childhood to the tearful conclusion, was fabricated."
- **Three Short + Long (The Drumbeat):** Three brief sentences create rhythm; a longer one releases it. "He stood. He waited. He breathed. Then the doors opened and the noise of the crowd hit him like a wall."

**Varying sentence openers (from Purdue OWL):**
- **Subject opener** (default): "The dog barked."
- **Adverb opener**: "Suddenly, the dog barked."
- **Prepositional phrase**: "In the dead of night, the dog barked."
- **Participial phrase** (sparingly): "Sensing danger, the dog barked."
- **Conjunction**: "But the dog didn't bark."
- **Question**: "Why did the dog bark?"
- **Inverted**: "Bark, the dog did."

Don't start three sentences in a row with the same structure. AI defaults to Subject-Verb-Object repeatedly — break this pattern.

**The rhythm reset:**
After a passage of complex sentences, drop in something simple. After a string of short declaratives, stretch into something longer. The variation itself is the craft.

### What to Avoid

- Five consecutive sentences between 15-25 words (the AI "comfort zone")
- Every paragraph starting with a topic sentence
- Every sentence following SVO order
- Sentence length that never drops below 8 words or exceeds 30

## Paragraph Craft

### The Paragraph as Unit of Thought

A paragraph develops ONE idea. Not states it — develops it. The difference:

**States (AI pattern):**
> Remote work has many benefits. It allows flexibility. Employees can work from anywhere. This leads to better work-life balance. Companies also save on office space.

**Develops (human pattern):**
> Remote work sounded like freedom until my kitchen table became my office, my commute became a hallway, and my "flexible schedule" became an always-on expectation. The benefits are real — I haven't worn pants with a button in three years — but so is the isolation. You don't realize how much you needed the small talk until it's gone.

### Paragraph Length Variation

AI produces paragraphs of similar length. Break this pattern:
- One-sentence paragraphs for emphasis or transitions
- Short paragraphs (2-3 sentences) for pace and energy
- Long paragraphs (6-8 sentences) for complex arguments or detailed scenes
- Vary deliberately based on what the content demands

### Paragraph Openings

Don't always lead with the main point. Options:
- **Scene-setting:** "The conference room was half-empty when the CEO walked in."
- **Concession:** "The data doesn't look great. But..."
- **Question:** "What happens when the system fails?"
- **Continuation:** Pick up directly from where the last paragraph ended
- **Contrast:** "Last quarter told a different story."
- **Buried lead:** Put the main point in the middle for emphasis through discovery

## Transitions: Natural vs. Mechanical

### The Problem

AI overuses explicit transition words: Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, However, Nevertheless, Consequently, In contrast, That being said, With that in mind.

These are legitimate in academic writing and formal reports. In most other contexts, they're crutches that replace genuine logical flow with mechanical connectors.

### The Five Transition Types (McCarl Framework)

**1. Echo links** — repeat a key term or concept from one paragraph to the next:
> End: "...once the commercial spaceflights begin."
> Start: "If they begin."

**2. Pointing words** — demonstrative adjectives (this, that, these, those) + a summary noun:
> "This connection reveals..." / "That assumption ignores..." / "These patterns suggest..."
> Rule: Never leave "this" orphaned. Always follow with a noun ("this *approach*", not just "this").

**3. Synonym bridges** — use related language rather than identical repetition:
> Don't say "tools" five times. Alternate: "instruments," "methods," "approaches."

**4. Paragraph hooks** — first sentence of a new paragraph echoes the previous paragraph's topic while introducing the new one:
> Previous paragraph discusses rising costs.
> Next opens: "Those costs, however, tell only half the story."

**5. Links to broader arguments** — orient readers toward the overarching theme:
> "Returning to the central question of scalability..."

### Additional Natural Transition Techniques

**Pronoun bridges:**
> We launched the new feature on Tuesday. It broke within an hour.

**Question-and-answer:**
> So what went wrong? The short answer: everything.

**Logical sequencing (the reader infers the connection):**
> Revenue dropped 15% in Q3. The board called an emergency meeting.

No transition word needed — the causal relationship is obvious.

**Contrast through juxtaposition (no connector needed):**
> The marketing team promised a "seamless launch." Engineering shipped with 47 known bugs.

**Temporal flow:**
> First came the prototype. Then the user tests. Then the uncomfortable silence in the review meeting.

**The "But Wait" subversion:**
> "We all age. Or so scientists thought." Present common knowledge, then subvert.

**Declarative signpost:**
> A short, plain statement signals a new direction. "Phages are viruses." No transition word needed.

**Delete-and-test:** Remove a transition word. If the paragraph still flows, the transition was unnecessary padding. Skilled writers transition through *content logic*, not connector words.

### When Explicit Transitions Are Appropriate

- Academic papers (expected by convention)
- Legal writing (precision matters)
- Technical documentation (step sequencing)
- Formal business reports (board-level communication)
- When the logical connection genuinely isn't obvious without one

Even in these contexts, vary the transitions. Don't use "Furthermore" three times on one page.

## Voice and Personality

### What Makes Writing Sound Human

Human writing has:
- **Opinions stated without full justification** — "The redesign is ugly" (not "The redesign arguably fails to meet certain aesthetic criteria")
- **Self-corrections** — "Actually, that's not quite right. What I mean is..."
- **Asides and tangents** — parenthetical thoughts, footnotes, dashes that break the flow
- **Humor that risks falling flat** — jokes, understatement, dry observation
- **Specificity from experience** — "The 3 AM Slack message that started everything" vs. "Communication challenges arose"
- **Imperfect structure** — not every argument is balanced; some points get more space because they matter more

### What Makes Writing Sound Like AI

- Every claim is hedged or qualified
- Every point is balanced with a counterpoint
- Emotional register never shifts
- Nothing is stated that could be controversial
- Perfect parallel structure everywhere
- No specificity from personal experience — all examples are generic
- Performed empathy: "This can be incredibly frustrating..."

### Injecting Personality Without Being Unprofessional

- Replace one generic example with a specific one from the actual situation
- Allow one opinion per section that isn't fully argued — just stated
- Let sentence fragments stand when they serve emphasis
- Use "I" or "we" instead of passive voice in at least some places
- Include one concrete detail that only someone involved would know

## Editing Checklist

When revising text (your own or AI-assisted), check for:

1. **Sentence length variety** — calculate SD if suspicious. Break up uniform passages.
2. **Paragraph length variety** — are they all the same size? Add a one-liner or expand one.
3. **Transition audit** — highlight all explicit transition words. Cut half of them. Do the connections still hold? If yes, they were crutches.
4. **Opening audit** — do 3+ sentences in a row start the same way? Restructure.
5. **Specificity pass** — replace one vague claim per paragraph with a concrete example, number, or name.
6. **Hedging pass** — cut every hedge that isn't earning its keep. "May potentially" → "may." "Could arguably" → state the argument.
7. **Read aloud** — if you can read it in a monotone without feeling weird, it needs more variation.
