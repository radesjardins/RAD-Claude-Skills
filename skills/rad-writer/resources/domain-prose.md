# Prose Writing Reference

## What Good Prose Writing Looks Like

### Sentence-Level Craft

#### Rhythm and Cadence
Good prose has rhythm. Not the mechanical rhythm of identically structured sentences, but the musical rhythm of variation — long sentences that build and layer and compound, followed by short ones that land.

The short sentence after a long one carries disproportionate weight. Use it for emphasis.

**Do this:**
"She had spent three years building the company from a two-person operation in a basement office to a sixty-person team with clients in fourteen countries, working through weekends and holidays and the kind of grinding uncertainty that makes most founders quit. Then she sold it for a dollar."

**Not that:**
"She had spent three years building the company. It went from a two-person operation to a sixty-person team. They had clients in fourteen countries. She worked through weekends and holidays. Then she sold it for a dollar."

The second version has no rhythm. Every sentence is the same length. The surprise at the end falls flat because there's no buildup to contrast against.

#### Sentence Length as a Tool
- **Short sentences (under 10 words):** Signal importance, create urgency, deliver punchlines. "He lied." "It worked." "Nobody came."
- **Medium sentences (10-25 words):** The workhorses. Carry explanation, narration, argument.
- **Long sentences (25+ words):** Build atmosphere, convey complexity, mirror the experience of accumulation or overwhelm. But they must be grammatically controlled — a long sentence that loses its thread is not stylistic, it's sloppy.

The pattern that creates the most power: medium, medium, long, short.

#### Right-Branching Sentences
Place the core subject and verb early, then let heavy modifying phrases branch to the right (the end of the sentence). Center-embedding — stuffing clauses in the middle between subject and verb — crashes the reader's working memory.

**Right-branching (readable):** "She resigned on Tuesday, after three years of declining morale, a leadership shuffle that left her team without a sponsor, and a final all-hands that confirmed her worst suspicions." (Rebecca Goldstein has demonstrated that even 340-word sentences remain readable when they branch right.)

**Center-embedded (unreadable):** "She, after three years of declining morale and a leadership shuffle that left her team without a sponsor and a final all-hands that confirmed her worst suspicions, resigned on Tuesday."

The right-branching sentence works because the reader gets the point ("She resigned") immediately and then accumulates context. The center-embedded version forces the reader to hold "She" in memory while processing a 30-word interruption before reaching the verb.

#### The "Given-Before-New" Principle
Start sentences with familiar information (what the reader already knows), end with the new information or payoff. This mirrors how the brain processes — it anchors on the known and absorbs the unknown.

**Do this:** "The server crashed at 9:02 AM. The crash triggered a cascade that took down three downstream services." (The crash is now "given" — the cascade is "new.")

**Not that:** "A cascade that took down three downstream services was triggered by the crash." (New info first, familiar info buried at the end.)

#### Sentence Openings
Vary how sentences begin. A paragraph where every sentence starts with the subject ("She went... She thought... She decided...") reads like a police report.

**Tools for variation:**
- Participial phrase: "Running through the numbers again, she noticed the discrepancy."
- Prepositional phrase: "In the third quarter, everything changed."
- Adverbial: "Quietly, without announcement, they shut it down."
- Inverted structure: "Gone was the optimism of January."
- Conjunction: "And then it broke." "But that's not the whole story."

Use these deliberately. A paragraph with five different openers feels show-offy. Two or three variations in a paragraph of six sentences is natural.

### Show vs. Tell in Non-Fiction

Show vs. tell applies to non-fiction just as much as fiction. The difference is between making an assertion and providing evidence that lets the reader reach the conclusion.

**Tell:** "The company culture was toxic."
**Show:** "In her first week, three people warned her not to eat lunch at her desk — not because of crumbs, but because the CEO took it as a sign you weren't 'committed to the team.' She ate in her car."

**Tell:** "The product launch was a disaster."
**Show:** "The server crashed at 9:02 AM, two minutes after the launch email went out to 40,000 subscribers. By noon, the hashtag #BrokenLaunch was trending. The CEO's apology tweet had a typo."

**Tell:** "He was a brilliant engineer."
**Show:** "He could read a stack trace the way a conductor reads a score — hearing the dissonance before anyone else noticed something was off."

The principle: abstraction is forgettable. Specificity is memorable. "The meeting went badly" gives the reader nothing. "The CFO closed his laptop mid-sentence and left" gives them a scene.

### Paragraph Development

#### The "Burrito Paragraph"
A paragraph should be tightly wrapped around a single topic — if you throw it, it shouldn't spill. This test reveals when a paragraph is trying to cover two ideas or when sentences have drifted from the controlling thought.

A paragraph is a unit of thought, not a unit of length. Each paragraph should:
1. Introduce one idea (topic sentence, explicit or implied)
2. Develop it (evidence, example, elaboration)
3. Land it (implication, transition, or resonance)

#### The "Pull-a-Sentence" Test
Remove any single sentence from a paragraph. If the paragraph still reads coherently, the removed sentence was probably filler. Every sentence in a paragraph should be load-bearing — contributing either evidence, transition, or emphasis that the paragraph cannot do without.

**The one-sentence paragraph** is a legitimate tool for emphasis. Don't overuse it, or it loses its power.

**Paragraph transitions** should be invisible. The best transition is a logical one — the reader moves from paragraph A to paragraph B because A created a question that B answers. Mechanical transitions ("Furthermore," "In addition," "Moreover") are crutches that signal weak structural logic.

**Do this:** End paragraph A with a tension or question. Begin paragraph B with the answer or development.

**Not that:** End every paragraph with a transitional sentence that announces what's coming next. "This brings us to the next important point: paragraph structure."

### Essay Structures

#### The Linear Argument
Thesis, evidence, evidence, evidence, conclusion. The classic academic structure. Works well for op-eds, position papers, and persuasive writing. Danger: predictability. The reader knows where you're going by paragraph two.

#### The Braided Essay
Two or more narrative threads that alternate and eventually converge. Thread A might be personal experience; Thread B might be research or history. The power comes from the moment they intersect.

**Example:** An essay about remote work that braids (A) the author's experience working from a cabin during COVID, (B) the history of telecommuting from the 1970s, and (C) new research on productivity and isolation. The threads converge when the author realizes their cabin isolation mirrors the failed 1970s experiment — and the research explains why.

#### The Lyric Essay
Fragments, white space, associative leaps. Prioritizes resonance over argument. The reader constructs meaning from juxtaposition rather than following a logical chain. Works for personal essays, cultural criticism, and meditative pieces. Requires confidence — a lyric essay that doesn't land feels pretentious.

#### The Hermit Crab Essay
Borrows the structure of another form: a recipe, a FAQ, a field guide, a Craigslist listing, a medical chart. The borrowed form creates ironic distance or reveals unexpected emotional depth.

**Example:** An essay about divorce structured as an IKEA assembly manual. An essay about grief structured as a recipe for the dish your mother made.

The form must earn its structure — if the borrowed form doesn't add meaning, it's a gimmick.

#### The Spiral
Returns to the same image, question, or scene multiple times, each time with more depth or a shifted perspective. Mimics how humans actually process difficult ideas — circling back, understanding more each time.

### Classic Style
Classic prose adopts the metaphor of "vision" — the writer directs the reader's gaze through a "window onto the world." The writer has seen something and is pointing it out. The reader is an equal, not a student. The prose is confident but not aggressive, clear but not dumbed down. Classic style avoids meta-commentary ("In this essay, I will argue...") because the writer is showing, not performing.

### The "So What?" Test
After writing any claim, keep asking "So what?" until you hit tangible human benefit. Abstract value chains are forgettable; concrete endpoints stick.

**The chain:** "We automated deployment workflows" -> So what? -> "Teams save 4 hours per week" -> So what? -> "Engineers leave the office by 6 PM" -> So what? -> "They make their kid's soccer game."

Stop when you hit something a person would actually feel. That's where persuasion lives.

### Voice and Tone Control

#### Serious Without Stiff
Formal writing doesn't mean humorless or robotic. The key is precision, not pomposity.

**Stiff:** "It is the opinion of this author that the aforementioned policy constitutes a significant departure from established precedent."
**Serious:** "This policy breaks with twenty years of precedent. That's either bold or reckless, depending on whether it works."

To be serious without stiffness: use active voice, use concrete nouns, avoid Latinate abstractions when Anglo-Saxon words exist ("use" not "utilize," "end" not "terminate," "help" not "facilitate").

#### Funny Without Flippant
Humor in prose earns trust — it signals the writer is human and in control. But humor that undermines the subject matter makes the reader doubt whether the writer takes anything seriously.

**Funny and effective:** "The committee met for four hours and produced a document that weighed more than any of its ideas."
**Flippant and undermining:** "LOL, the committee basically wasted everyone's time with their dumb report."

Humor should illuminate, not dismiss. The best prose humor comes from observation and specificity, not from signaling that you think the subject is beneath you.

#### Calibrating Register
Every piece has a register — the level of formality and intimacy. A personal essay in the New Yorker occupies a different register than a Medium post, which occupies a different register than a private journal entry.

Signs you're in the wrong register:
- Using contractions in a formal research paper
- Avoiding all contractions in a blog post (reads as robotic)
- First person in a policy brief
- Third person in a personal essay
- Slang in a commencement address
- No humor in a 5,000-word profile piece

### Editing Techniques: What to Cut

#### Adverbs
Not all adverbs are bad. But adverbs that duplicate what the verb already implies are dead weight.

**Cut:** "She shouted loudly." (Shouting is loud.)
**Cut:** "He ran quickly." (Running is quick. If you mean sprinting, say sprinting.)
**Keep:** "She said softly." (Said doesn't imply volume; softly adds real information.)
**Keep:** "He walked slowly." (Walking doesn't imply pace; slowly is doing work.)

#### "Shudder Quotes"
Don't use quotation marks to distance yourself from word choices. "The team was 'restructured'" signals the writer doesn't want to own the word. If "restructured" is the right word, use it without quotes. If it's euphemistic and you want to call that out, say so directly: "The team was fired — though the memo called it a restructuring."

Scare quotes are a hedge. They say "I'm using this word, but I want you to know I'm too sophisticated to really mean it." Commit to your word choices or choose different words.

#### Zombie Nouns (Nominalizations)
Verbs suffocated into abstract nouns — typically with -tion, -ment, -ance, -ence suffixes. They drain sentences of energy and agency.

**Zombie:** "The proliferation of nominalizations in a sentence is an indication of a tendency toward abstraction."
**Alive:** "Writers who overload sentences with abstract nouns lose their readers."

**Zombie:** "The implementation of the recommendation led to an improvement in performance."
**Alive:** "We implemented their recommendation, and performance improved."

The fix: find the noun, find the verb hiding inside it, and let the verb do the work.

#### Qualifiers and Hedges
"Very," "really," "quite," "somewhat," "rather," "fairly," "slightly" — these words weaken whatever follows them.

**Cut:** "The results were very impressive." -> "The results were impressive." (Or better: show the results and let the reader decide.)
**Cut:** "It's quite clear that..." -> "It's clear that..." -> even better: just state the thing that's clear.

The exception: when the qualifier is genuinely doing calibration work. "She was slightly annoyed" is meaningfully different from "She was annoyed." Keep it when it changes the meaning.

#### Throat-Clearing
The first sentence or paragraph of a draft is often throat-clearing — the writer warming up. The real essay usually starts in paragraph two or three.

**Common throat-clears:**
- "In today's world..." / "In our modern society..."
- "It goes without saying that..." (Then don't say it.)
- "Have you ever wondered...?" (Rhetorical questions as openers rarely earn their keep.)
- "Since the dawn of time..." / "Throughout history..." (Almost never true, and never specific.)
- "Webster's dictionary defines X as..."

Cut the throat-clearing. Find where the writing gets specific and start there.

#### Redundancies
- "Past history" (all history is past)
- "Future plans" (all plans are future)
- "Unexpected surprise" (all surprises are unexpected)
- "End result" (results come at the end)
- "Basic fundamentals" (fundamentals are basic by definition)
- "Collaborate together" (collaboration is together by definition)
- "Advance planning" (planning is in advance by definition)

#### That
The word "that" is necessary less often than writers think. Read the sentence without it. If it's clear, cut it.

**Before:** "She said that the project was on track."
**After:** "She said the project was on track."

Keep "that" when removing it creates ambiguity or a garden-path sentence.

#### Reading Aloud for "Garden Path" Sentences
Read your prose aloud to detect garden-path sentences — phrases that lead the brain down the wrong syntactic parse before forcing a restart. Classic example: "The horse raced past the barn fell." The reader processes "raced" as the main verb, then hits "fell" and has to re-parse the entire sentence.

Reading aloud catches these because the ear stumbles where the eye skims. If you trip over a sentence while reading aloud, your reader will trip over it silently.

#### The Power of Extreme Brevity
Short standalone sentences — even standalone paragraphs — carry enormous weight when they follow rich, layered context. Isabel Wilkerson ends a chapter rich with the Great Migration's complexity with a two-syllable standalone paragraph: "They left." The brevity becomes the emphasis. This technique works because the surrounding density creates the contrast.

### Essay Openings That Land

#### The Scene
Drop the reader into a specific moment with sensory detail. Not a summary, not context — a scene.

**Do this:** "The server room was 62 degrees and humming. Martinez knelt in front of rack 14, the one with the blinking amber light, and pulled the drive."

**Not that:** "In the world of data center management, hardware failures are a common occurrence."

#### The Counterintuitive Claim
State something that challenges the reader's assumption. Make them need to keep reading.

**Do this:** "The best product managers are the ones who ship nothing."

**Not that:** "Product management is a challenging but rewarding field."

#### The Telling Detail
One specific detail that implies a larger truth.

**Do this:** "The company's Glassdoor page had fourteen reviews. Twelve were posted on the same Tuesday afternoon."

#### The Direct Address
Speak to the reader as if you know them.

**Do this:** "You've had this meeting. The one where everyone agrees the project is on track while privately knowing it's on fire."

### Essay Endings That Land

#### The Callback
Return to your opening image, scene, or detail — but now it means something different because of what you've argued or revealed.

#### The Widening
Move from the specific to the universal. The essay was about one company, one person, one decision — but the ending makes clear it's about all of us.

#### The Question Left Ringing
Don't answer everything. End with a question that the reader will carry with them. Not a rhetorical question that has an obvious answer — a genuine one.

#### The Last Image
End on something concrete. Not an abstraction, not a lesson, not a moral — an image. "She closed the laptop and walked out into the parking lot. It was still light out."

## Common Mistakes

1. **Burying the lede.** The most interesting, provocative, or important thing is in paragraph four. Move it to paragraph one.
2. **Uniform sentence length.** Every sentence is 15-20 words. The prose flatlines. Vary. Dramatically.
3. **Telling the reader how to feel.** "This heartbreaking story..." / "In a hilarious turn of events..." If you have to label the emotion, the writing isn't evoking it.
4. **Over-signposting.** "First, I'll discuss... Then, I'll examine... Finally, I'll argue..." This is outline formatting, not prose.
5. **Cliche clusters.** "At the end of the day, it is what it is, and we need to think outside the box." Every cliche is a place where the writer stopped thinking.
6. **Passive voice as default.** "The decision was made." By whom? Passive voice removes agency and accountability. Use it deliberately or not at all.
7. **Ending with a summary.** "In conclusion, this essay has argued that..." The reader just read the essay. They don't need a recap. End with resonance.
8. **Abstract pile-up.** "The implementation of strategic initiatives for organizational transformation requires stakeholder alignment." Replace every abstract noun with a concrete one. What initiatives? What transformation? Which stakeholders?

## Context-Gathering Questions

Before writing prose, clarify:

1. **What is the publication or platform?** A New Yorker essay has different expectations than a Substack post.
2. **Who is the reader?** Specialist or general audience? Sympathetic or skeptical?
3. **What is the word count?** 800 words and 5,000 words are fundamentally different forms.
4. **What is the essay's argument or emotional core?** State it in one sentence.
5. **What is the reader's starting assumption?** And where do you want them to end up?
6. **What is the register?** Formal academic? Conversational? Literary? Journalistic?
7. **Are there structural constraints?** Op-ed conventions? Section requirements?
8. **First person, second person, or third person?** And does it shift?
9. **What research or reporting supports this?** Essays built on assertion alone are thin.
10. **What is the one sentence you'd put on the back cover?** This clarifies the core before you start writing.

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] The opening earns the reader's attention in the first two sentences
- [ ] Sentence length varies deliberately — short sentences carry emphasis
- [ ] The essay shows rather than tells at key moments
- [ ] Each paragraph contains one developed idea
- [ ] Transitions are logical, not mechanical ("Furthermore," "Moreover")
- [ ] Adverbs and qualifiers are cut unless doing genuine calibration work
- [ ] The voice is consistent in register throughout
- [ ] No throat-clearing — the essay starts where the writing gets specific
- [ ] The ending resonates rather than summarizes
- [ ] The essay has a structure (braided, linear, spiral, etc.) that serves the content
- [ ] Passive voice is used deliberately, not as a default
- [ ] Every paragraph could answer: "Why is this here? What does it add?"

## AI Pattern Considerations

AI-generated prose tends to:

1. **Flatten rhythm.** AI writes in uniform medium-length sentences. There's no short punch after a long build. Every sentence is 15-22 words. The result reads like a well-organized report, not like prose.
2. **Over-qualify.** "This can potentially be seen as somewhat problematic." AI hedges because it's trained on hedging. Good prose commits.
3. **Avoid the first person.** AI defaults to distant, authoritative third person even when the piece calls for intimacy. If the essay needs "I," use "I."
4. **Reach for cliches.** "At the end of the day," "a double-edged sword," "the tip of the iceberg," "it goes without saying." These are the phrases AI reaches for because they appeared millions of times in training data.
5. **Summarize instead of scene-set.** AI tells you what happened. Good prose shows you what happened. AI writes "The meeting was tense." A human writer writes the moment someone slammed their notebook shut.
6. **Use symmetrical structure compulsively.** Three examples, each with three sentences. Four points, each the same length. Real essays have asymmetric emphasis — one example gets a full paragraph, another gets one sentence.
7. **Front-load context.** AI spends the first 20% of any piece establishing background, defining terms, and setting the stage. Most of this is throat-clearing. Cut to the tension.
8. **Avoid genuine opinion.** AI prose tends toward balanced, even-handed analysis. Prose with a point of view takes a stand and defends it. "Both sides have valid points" is not an essay — it's a Wikipedia article.
9. **Use transition words as paragraph openers.** "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover," "In contrast." These are neon signs that say "AI wrote this." Good transitions are structural, not lexical.
10. **End with uplift.** AI almost always ends on an optimistic, forward-looking note. Sometimes the honest ending is ambiguous, unresolved, or dark. Let the content dictate the ending, not a bias toward positivity.
11. **Produce "correct" but lifeless metaphors.** "A beacon of hope," "navigating the landscape," "the fabric of society." AI metaphors are technically applicable but worn smooth from overuse. A good metaphor surprises — it connects two things the reader hasn't connected before.
12. **Write topic sentences that announce rather than argue.** "There are several reasons why this matters." That's a table of contents entry, not a topic sentence. A good topic sentence makes a claim: "The real cost isn't financial — it's the trust that doesn't come back."
