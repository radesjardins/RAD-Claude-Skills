# Presentation Writing Reference

## What Good Presentation Writing Looks Like

### The Billboard Test
Every slide should pass the billboard test: if someone driving past at 60 mph could not grasp the point in 3 seconds, the slide has too much on it. Presentation slides are a visual medium, not a document delivery format.

**Do this:** One idea per slide. Six words or fewer for a headline. A single image or chart that reinforces the point.

**Not that:** Three bullet points each containing a full sentence. A chart with 12 data series. A paragraph of text the speaker reads aloud.

### The 6x6 Rule
No more than 6 lines per slide, no more than 6 words per line. This ceiling forces compression. Combined with the 10/20/30 Rule (minimum 30pt font), these constraints force ruthless editing — if the text won't fit at 30pt, cut the text, not the font size.

### Fragments Over Sentences
Slide text should use fragments, not complete sentences. Sentences invite reading. Fragments invite listening.

**Do this:**
- "Revenue: up 23%"
- "3 blockers remain"
- "Ship date: March 15"

**Not that:**
- "Our revenue has increased by 23% compared to the same quarter last year."
- "There are currently three blockers that remain unresolved on the project."
- "We are targeting March 15 as our ship date for the next release."

### Speaker Notes Are the Real Writing
The craft of presentation writing lives in the speaker notes, not the slides. Notes should be:
- Conversational, not scripted (bullet prompts, not paragraphs to read verbatim)
- Structured with transition phrases ("This brings us to...", "So what does this mean?")
- Timed (mark approximate minute markers for rehearsal)
- Inclusive of backup data the speaker might need if questioned

**Writing process for notes:** Write complete thoughts first, then edit down to a bulleted outline with **bold keywords**. This preserves the logic while removing the temptation to read verbatim.

**Question-Based Prompts:** Frame notes as questions rather than statements. "What is the cost of delay?" forces conversational delivery. "The cost of delay is $2M/quarter" invites reading aloud. Questions engage the speaker's own thinking and produce more natural speech.

**Do this:** "Transition: 'So we know the what. Let's talk about the why.' Key stat: 73% of customers cited speed as #1 factor. Anecdote: Acme Corp deal — they switched after a 2-second load time improvement."

**Not that:** Write the entire speech as a paragraph the presenter will read word-for-word from their laptop screen.

### Rehearsal: "Measure Twice, Cut Once"
Three-pass rehearsal method:
1. **Read aloud once** — from the full notes, catching awkward phrasing and timing issues
2. **Rehearse without notes once** — using only the slides as prompts, identifying which points you remember naturally (those are the strong ones)
3. **Deliver without script** — speak from knowledge, not from memory. If you can't deliver a point without notes, you don't understand it well enough yet

### Narrative Structure
The strongest presentations follow situation-complication-resolution (SCR), not topic-subtopic-subtopic.

**Situation:** Establish shared reality. "We launched v2 in January. Adoption hit 40% by March."
**Complication:** Introduce tension. "But churn doubled in Q2. Exit surveys pointed to onboarding friction."
**Resolution:** Deliver the answer. "We're proposing a guided setup flow. Here's the prototype and timeline."

This structure works because it mirrors how humans process information: context, problem, solution. It creates forward momentum — the audience wants to know what happens next.

#### Context-Specific Narrative Frameworks
- **Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR):** The consulting standard for strategy presentations. Works when the audience needs to understand a problem before accepting a solution.
- **Problem-Solution-Benefit:** The sales structure. Spend 70% on the customer's challenge, 30% on the product. The audience should feel their pain described more precisely than they could articulate it themselves.
- **Freytag's Pyramid:** For training and change management decks. Build tension around the need for change before presenting the new path. Stories following this arc produce roughly 40% higher retention than pure data presentations because tension creates engagement.

### Opening Techniques That Work
1. **The startling fact:** "47% of your users abandon your product before they finish setup." (Data that reframes assumptions.)
2. **The question:** "What would you do with 3 extra hours every week?" (Engages the audience mentally before you speak.)
3. **The story:** "Last Tuesday, a customer called our support line in tears." (Emotional specificity beats abstraction.)
4. **The demonstration:** Show the product, the problem, the before/after. No preamble.

**Openings that fail:**
- "Today I'm going to talk about..." (Agenda slides as openers kill energy.)
- "Let me introduce myself..." (Unless the audience genuinely doesn't know you and your credibility matters for the argument.)
- "Sorry, I'm not great at presentations..." (Self-deprecation as an opener signals the audience should lower expectations.)

### Closing Techniques That Land
1. **The callback:** Return to your opening story or fact with new meaning. "Remember that customer who called in tears? Here's what she said after using the new flow."
2. **The single ask:** "I need one thing from this group: approval to staff two engineers on this for the next sprint."
3. **The provocative reframe:** "We're not choosing between features. We're choosing what kind of company we want to be."

**Closings that fail:**
- "Any questions?" as the final slide. (End with your point, then take questions.)
- "That's it!" or "I think that's everything." (Anti-climactic. The ending should feel intentional.)
- A summary slide that repeats every point. (If you need this, your presentation was too long or unfocused.)

## Context-Specific Expectations

### Keynote / Conference Talk
- Heavy on story, light on data density
- Slides are cinematic: full-bleed images, minimal text, dramatic reveals
- Speaker notes carry 90% of the content
- 1 slide per 1-2 minutes typical pacing
- Audience is there voluntarily — earn and keep their attention

### Team Update / Sprint Review
- Information-dense but scannable
- Charts, metrics, status indicators (green/yellow/red)
- Speaker notes are lighter — the team has context
- Can include more text per slide because the audience is engaged and motivated
- End with blockers and asks, not applause lines

### Board Meeting / Executive Review
- Lead with the ask or the decision needed, not the backstory
- Recommendation in the first 60 seconds — executives decide quickly and spend the rest validating
- Appendix slides for detail — keep the main deck to 6-8 slides for a 15-minute slot, with an appendix for depth
- Use the "newspaper test": could an executive skim the headlines and get 80% of the story?
- Financial data should be pre-read; the presentation is for discussion, not revelation
- Anticipate the three hardest questions and build hidden slides for them

### Sales Pitch / Client Presentation
- Structure around the client's problem, not your product's features
- "You" language outnumbers "we" language 3:1
- Social proof (logos, quotes, case studies) belongs early, not as an afterthought
- Demo or prototype beats a description every time
- The close must include a specific next step with a date

## Data Presentation in Slides

### Chart Discipline
- One message per chart. If a chart supports two arguments, make two charts.
- Title the chart with the insight, not the category: "Revenue grew 23% YoY" not "Revenue by Quarter"
- Remove chartjunk: gridlines, 3D effects, legends when you can label directly, decimal places beyond what matters
- Use color to highlight the data point that matters; gray out everything else
- If the audience needs 10 seconds to decode the chart, it's too complex for a slide

### Action Titles on Data Slides
Use complete-sentence titles (under 15 words) that state the conclusion, not the topic. These are "action titles" — they tell the audience what to think about the data.

**Do this:** "Enterprise deals drove 80% of Q3 growth" (action title — states the insight)
**Not that:** "Q3 Revenue by Segment" (topic title — forces the audience to figure out the insight themselves)

### Data Annotations
Add annotations directly on charts to guide interpretation:
- **Observational annotations:** State what happened. "Sales dropped 23% in March."
- **Additive annotations:** Explain why. "Factory closed due to supply shortage."

Observational annotations confirm what the audience sees. Additive annotations provide context they cannot see. Use both together for maximum clarity.

**Do this:** A simple bar chart with one bar highlighted in brand color, titled "Enterprise deals drove 80% of Q3 growth"

**Not that:** A stacked bar chart with 8 color-coded segments, a legend in 8pt font, gridlines at every $50K increment, titled "Revenue Breakdown"

### Tables in Slides
Tables almost never work in presentations. If you must use one:
- Maximum 4 columns, 5 rows
- Bold the row or cell that matters
- Use it for comparison, not raw data display

### The "So What?" Test
Every data slide must answer: "So what?" If the slide shows a metric, the headline or speaker note must explain why the audience should care.

**Do this:** Slide headline: "Churn is accelerating — we'll lose $2M ARR by Q4 at this rate." Chart shows the trend.

**Not that:** Slide headline: "Monthly Churn Rate." Chart shows the same trend. Audience thinks, "Is this good? Bad? What am I supposed to do with this?"

## Bullet Point Discipline

### The Rule of Three
Three bullets is the ideal maximum. Five is the absolute ceiling. If you have more than five, you have a list slide that should be restructured as:
- Multiple slides (one per point with a visual)
- A handout (not a slide)
- A build sequence (reveal one at a time)

### Parallel Structure
Bullets must be grammatically parallel. If the first starts with a verb, they all start with verbs. If the first is a noun phrase, they're all noun phrases.

**Do this:**
- Identify the root cause
- Design the solution
- Validate with users

**Not that:**
- Root cause identification
- We need to design a solution
- Validating with users is important

### Progressive Disclosure
In live presentations, build bullets one at a time. The audience reads ahead if you show all bullets simultaneously, which means they stop listening to you.

## The Slideument Problem

A "slideument" is a text-heavy hybrid that tries to function as both a visual aid and a standalone report — and fails at both jobs. As a document, it lacks narrative flow and proper formatting. As a presentation, the audience reads instead of listens. The slideument is the single most common failure mode in corporate decks.

**The fix:** Create two artifacts.
1. A presentation deck (visual, minimal text, designed for live delivery)
2. A leave-behind document (full narrative, detailed data, designed for asynchronous reading)

If stakeholders insist on "sending the deck around," create the leave-behind as a separate document. Appendix slides in the deck can bridge the gap for smaller asks.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Reading slides aloud.** If the slide says it, the speaker shouldn't. The slide and the speaker should complement each other, not duplicate.
2. **Animation abuse.** Transitions and animations should be invisible. If the audience notices the animation, it's distracting from the message.
3. **Logo-heavy title slides.** Three logos, a date, a subtitle, a conference name, a hashtag. The title slide should set the tone, not serve as a legal filing.
4. **The apology opener.** "I know this is a lot of slides..." Then cut slides. Apologizing for your own content signals you didn't prepare well enough.
5. **Orphan slides.** A slide that doesn't connect to the one before or after it. Every slide needs a transition — spoken or structural.
6. **Treating Q&A as the end.** Q&A is a discussion, not a conclusion. Have a closing slide ready for after Q&A to end on your terms.
7. **Inconsistent visual language.** Mixing fonts, color palettes, chart styles, and image treatments across slides. A deck should look like one person made it.
8. **The wall of logos.** A "customers" or "partners" slide with 40 tiny logos. Pick 5-8 that your audience will recognize and respect. Less is more.

## Context-Gathering Questions

Before writing any presentation, clarify:

1. **Who is the audience?** Role, seniority, technical depth, what they already know
2. **What is the one thing they should remember?** If they forget everything else, what sticks?
3. **What is the ask?** Approval, budget, headcount, alignment, awareness?
4. **What is the setting?** In-person, virtual, hybrid? Large room or small meeting?
5. **How long is the slot?** And how much of that is Q&A?
6. **Will the deck be sent ahead or after?** This determines whether you need a leave-behind version
7. **What's the emotional state of the audience?** Excited? Skeptical? Exhausted from three prior presentations?
8. **What are the three objections they'll raise?** Build pre-emptive answers into the flow
9. **Is there a template or brand constraint?** Corporate decks often have mandatory layouts
10. **Who presents?** The writer and presenter may be different people. Calibrate formality and speaker notes accordingly.

## Quality Criteria

- [ ] Every slide passes the billboard test (readable in 3 seconds)
- [ ] The deck follows SCR or another clear narrative arc
- [ ] No slide has more than 5 bullet points
- [ ] Bullets are parallel in grammatical structure
- [ ] Data slides are titled with the insight, not the metric name
- [ ] The opening hooks attention in the first 15 seconds
- [ ] The closing includes a clear ask or memorable reframe
- [ ] Speaker notes contain transitions between every slide
- [ ] The deck works without the speaker (leave-behind version) OR is clearly designed for live-only delivery
- [ ] Consistent visual language: fonts, colors, image treatment
- [ ] Total slide count fits the time slot (roughly 1-2 minutes per slide for keynotes, faster for updates)
- [ ] No orphan slides — every slide connects to what comes before and after

## AI Pattern Considerations

AI-generated presentations tend to:

1. **Over-structure.** Every deck gets an agenda slide, a summary slide, and a "thank you" slide regardless of context. A 5-slide team update does not need an agenda.
2. **Default to five bullets per slide.** AI loves the five-point list. Push for fewer, punchier points.
3. **Use generic stock imagery language.** "Image of diverse team collaborating" is not a creative direction. Specify the exact visual or use a real photo.
4. **Write complete sentences as bullets.** AI defaults to grammatically complete thoughts. Rewrite as fragments.
5. **Produce slideuments.** AI packs information density appropriate for a document into a slide format. Cut aggressively.
6. **Use hedge language.** "This could potentially help improve outcomes" instead of "This will cut churn by 15%." Presentations demand confidence.
7. **Homogenize tone across contexts.** A board presentation and a conference keynote sound identical. They shouldn't.
8. **Skip emotional resonance.** AI-written presentations inform but rarely move. Add a story, a moment of tension, or a provocation that a human would naturally include.
9. **Overuse transitional phrases.** "Now let's move on to..." appears between every slide. Vary transitions or let the visual flow do the work.
10. **Create symmetric structures.** Three points, each with three sub-points, each with three examples. Real presentations have asymmetric emphasis — some points deserve five minutes, others deserve one.
