# Email Writing Reference

## Tone Calibration by Context

### Cold Outreach
- **Goal**: Earn 10 more seconds of attention, not close a deal.
- **Tone**: Respectful of their time, specific about why *them*, low-pressure.
- **Do this**: "I noticed your team shipped [specific thing] — we built [tool] that solves [problem that implies]. Worth a 15-min look?"
- **Not that**: "I'd love to pick your brain" / "I'm reaching out because..." / "Hope this email finds you well"
- Opening line should reference something *specific* about the recipient — a recent post, a company announcement, a shared connection. Generic flattery ("I'm a huge fan of your work") signals a mass blast.
- Keep to 4-6 sentences max. Every sentence must earn the next.
- End with a single, low-friction CTA: "Would Tuesday or Thursday work for a 15-min call?" not "Let me know if you'd be interested in learning more about how we could potentially help."

### Colleague / Peer
- **Goal**: Move work forward with minimal friction.
- **Tone**: Direct, warm but efficient. Skip pleasantries beyond one line.
- **Do this**: "Quick question on the Q3 dashboard — are we pulling from the warehouse or the API? Need to know before I wire up the pipeline."
- **Not that**: "Hey! Hope you had a great weekend! I was wondering if you might have a moment to chat about something I've been thinking about regarding the dashboard project..."
- Front-load the ask. If they need to scroll to find what you want, you've already lost.
- Use bullet points for multi-part questions. Numbered lists if order matters.

### Executive / Up-the-Chain
- **Goal**: Enable a decision or convey status without wasting their time.
- **Tone**: Confident, concise, structured. Lead with the conclusion.
- **Do this**: "Recommendation: Delay the launch by 2 weeks. The payment integration has 3 unresolved edge cases that would affect ~12% of transactions. Details below if needed."
- **Not that**: "I wanted to give you an update on where we are with the project. As you know, we've been working on the payment integration, and there have been some challenges..."
- Use the inverted pyramid: conclusion first, supporting evidence second, background last.
- Never bury bad news. If there's a problem, lead with it and your proposed solution.
- Bold or bullet the key decision/action if the email is more than 3 sentences.

### Customer-Facing
- **Goal**: Maintain trust and move toward resolution.
- **Tone**: Empathetic but not groveling. Professional warmth.
- **Do this**: "I understand the billing discrepancy is frustrating. Here's what happened and what we're doing about it: [specifics]."
- **Not that**: "We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused. Your satisfaction is our top priority."
- Acknowledge the impact *on them*, not just the problem in the abstract.
- Give concrete next steps with timelines: "We'll issue the credit by Friday" not "We'll look into this."
- Avoid corporate deflection language: "We're working to improve" means nothing without specifics.

### Delivering Bad News
- **Goal**: Be honest, preserve the relationship, provide a path forward.
- **Tone**: Direct but humane. Don't soften to the point of confusion.
- **Do this**: "We won't be able to meet the March 15 deadline. The API dependency we flagged in January turned out to need a full rewrite. New target: April 1. Here's the adjusted plan."
- **Not that**: "Unfortunately, due to unforeseen circumstances beyond our control, there may be a slight delay in the originally anticipated timeline..."
- State the news clearly in the first 1-2 sentences. Don't make them hunt for it.
- Own what's yours. Explain the cause without making excuses.
- Always pair bad news with a concrete plan or alternative.
- Never use "slight" or "minor" to minimize genuinely significant issues.
- **Use the "Past-Present-Future" structure**, not the compliment sandwich. Compliment sandwiches are transparent and patronizing. Instead: Past (what led here) -> Present (the bad news, stated plainly) -> Future (the path forward). This respects the reader's intelligence.

### Making Requests
- **Goal**: Get a yes, or at least a clear response.
- **Tone**: Respectful of their autonomy, specific about what you need and when.
- **Do this**: "Could you review the attached contract and flag any concerns by Thursday? The client needs our response by Friday EOD."
- **Not that**: "If you get a chance, it would be great if you could take a look at this when you have a moment."
- Include: what you need, why you need it, when you need it by, and what happens if the deadline slips.
- Make it easy to say yes: attach the document, include the link, pre-fill what you can.

### Internal Announcements
- **Goal**: Inform, align, and (sometimes) inspire.
- **Tone**: Clear and human. Match the weight of the news.
- **Do this**: "Starting March 1, we're moving to biweekly sprints. Here's why, what changes, and what stays the same."
- **Not that**: "Exciting news! We're thrilled to announce a transformative change to our agile methodology..."
- Don't oversell routine changes. "Exciting" is for genuinely exciting things.
- Structure: What's changing -> Why -> What it means for you -> When -> Who to ask questions.

## Subject Line Patterns

### What works
- **Specific + actionable**: "Q3 budget approval needed by Friday"
- **Benefit-forward**: "Your talk proposal: 3 suggestions to strengthen it"
- **Honest preview**: "Bad news on the Acme timeline — and a recovery plan"
- **Question format** (when genuine): "Can we move the standup to 10am?"
- **Bracket tags** for skimmability: "[Action needed] Design review for homepage redesign"

### What the data says
- 47% of recipients decide whether to open based on the subject line alone — it is the single highest-leverage sentence in the email.
- **Shorter wins on mobile**: 1-5 word subject lines (under 50 characters) get the highest open rates on mobile devices.
- **Numbers boost opens**: Including numbers in subject lines increases open rates by 45-57%.
- **Questions exploit the curiosity gap**: Question-format subject lines generate ~46% open rate because the reader needs the answer.
- **Personalization matters**: Using the recipient's first name in the subject line increases open rates 26-50%.

### What fails
- Vague: "Quick question" / "Following up" / "Checking in" / "Update"
- Clickbait: "You won't believe what happened with the deployment"
- ALL CAPS or excessive punctuation: "URGENT!!!" (unless the building is actually on fire)
- Puns or jokes that obscure the content: "Let's taco 'bout the new menu feature"
- Overly long: subject lines over 60 characters get truncated on mobile

### Subject line formula
`[Context tag] + Specific topic + Implied action or stakes`
Example: "[Decision needed] Vendor choice for auth service — $15K/yr difference"

## Opening Line Mistakes and Alternatives

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---|---|
| "Hope this email finds you well" | Just start with your point |
| "Per my last email" | "Following up on [specific thing]" |
| "I hope you had a great weekend" | Skip it, or reference something specific: "Saw your team's launch — congrats" |
| "As per our conversation" | "After our call on Tuesday, here's..." |
| "I'm reaching out because" | State your actual reason directly |
| "I wanted to touch base about" | "Two questions about the migration:" |
| "Sorry to bother you" | Don't apologize for communicating; just be concise |
| "Just circling back" | "The proposal deadline is Friday — do you have feedback on Section 3?" |

## Structure That Drives Action

### The BLUF Pattern (Bottom Line Up Front)
1. **First sentence**: The conclusion, recommendation, or request.
2. **Second section**: The 2-3 key supporting points.
3. **Third section**: Background, context, details (optional — many won't read this far).
4. **Final line**: Clear next step with a deadline.

### F-Pattern Scanning
Eye-tracking research shows readers scan emails in an F-shape: they read the first line or two fully, then scan down the left edge. If the action or ask isn't visually obvious within this pattern, they bounce. Place your key request or conclusion where the F-pattern puts the reader's eyes.

### Formatting for Skimmability
- One idea per paragraph. Three sentences max per paragraph.
- Use bold for the single most important phrase in a long email.
- Numbered lists for sequential steps. Bullets for parallel items.
- If your email exceeds 10 lines, add a one-sentence summary at the top.
- Use whitespace. Dense blocks of text signal "this will take effort to parse."

### The One-Screen Rule
If the recipient needs to scroll on mobile to find your ask, restructure. Put the ask in the first visible screen. Context goes below.

## Length Guidelines

| Context | Target length | Max length |
|---|---|---|
| Cold outreach | 3-5 sentences | 8 sentences |
| Quick question to peer | 1-3 sentences | 5 sentences |
| Status update | 5-10 sentences | 15 sentences with formatting |
| Executive summary | 3-5 sentences + bullets | 1 screen |
| Bad news delivery | 5-8 sentences | 12 sentences |
| Detailed proposal | N/A | Attach a doc; email is the cover note |

**Rule of thumb**: If you're writing more than 300 words, it should probably be a document with a short email linking to it.

### The 50-125 Word Sweet Spot
Data from 40M+ emails shows that emails between 50 and 125 words achieve the highest response rates. Writing at a 3rd-grade reading level (short words, simple sentences) correlates with 36% higher open rates and 53% response rates. Emails containing 1-3 questions are 50% more likely to get a reply than emails with zero questions.

## Cultural and Context Considerations

- **Formality spectrum**: Know where your recipient sits. "Hey!" to a Japanese executive is not the same as "Hey!" to your college roommate.
- **Direct vs. indirect cultures**: In some cultures, stating a request bluntly in the first line is rude. In others, burying it is disrespectful of their time. When in doubt, match their style from previous correspondence.
- **Time zone awareness**: "EOD" means nothing without a time zone. Specify: "by 5pm ET Friday."
- **Reply-all etiquette**: Default to reply (not reply-all) unless everyone on the thread genuinely needs your response.
- **CC vs. BCC**: CC means "FYI, this person is in the loop." BCC is for mass sends where recipients shouldn't see each other, or for quietly looping someone out of a thread.
- **Mobile-first**: Over 60% of business emails are first opened on mobile. Short paragraphs and clear formatting matter even more.

## Passive-Aggressive Patterns to Avoid

These phrases *sound* professional but communicate hostility:

| Passive-aggressive | What it really says | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Per my last email..." | "Can you not read?" | "To build on my earlier note about X..." |
| "As previously stated..." | "I already told you this." | Restate the relevant point without the preamble |
| "Going forward..." | "Don't mess this up again." | "For the next sprint, let's..." |
| "Thanks in advance" | "You don't have a choice." | "Would you be able to...? Thanks!" |
| "Please advise" | "Fix this." Reads as passive-aggressive in most contexts. | "What would you recommend?" or "Let me know what you think" |
| "Not sure if you saw my last email" | "I know you're ignoring me." | "Following up — do you have what you need to move forward on X?" |
| "Friendly reminder" | "This is not friendly." | "Quick reminder: the deadline for X is Friday." |
| "I've copied [manager] for visibility" | "I'm escalating." CC Warfare — copying managers to pressure people — destroys trust. | If you're escalating, be honest about why. If you're CC'ing for genuine visibility, say so explicitly: "Looping in [name] since this touches their budget." |
| "Correct me if I'm wrong, but..." | "I'm right and you're wrong." | State your understanding and ask for confirmation |

## Context-Gathering Questions

Before writing any email, ask:
1. **Who is the recipient?** Role, relationship to sender, communication preferences.
2. **What is the one thing they should do after reading this?** If you can't answer this, the email isn't ready.
3. **What do they already know?** Don't re-explain context they have. Don't assume context they don't.
4. **What's the emotional temperature?** Are they frustrated? Busy? Expecting this? Blindsided?
5. **Is email the right channel?** Would a Slack message, phone call, or in-person conversation be more effective?
6. **Who else is on the thread?** Adjust tone and detail level for the full audience, not just the primary recipient.
7. **What's the urgency?** Real urgency (client deadline tomorrow) vs. manufactured urgency (you want a quick reply).

## Quality Criteria for Email Review

- [ ] The subject line accurately previews the content and implies the needed action
- [ ] The first two sentences contain the core message or ask
- [ ] Every paragraph earns its place — no filler, no throat-clearing
- [ ] The ask is specific, with a clear deadline and any needed context
- [ ] Tone matches the relationship and situation
- [ ] No passive-aggressive phrasing
- [ ] Formatting aids scanning (bullets, bold, whitespace) for emails over 5 lines
- [ ] The email passes the "one screen on mobile" test for the critical content
- [ ] CC list is intentional, not reflexive
- [ ] Attachments are referenced in the body and actually attached
- [ ] If it's a reply, it addresses all points from the previous email

## AI Pattern Considerations

### Common AI tells in email writing
- **Over-hedging**: "I wanted to reach out to see if perhaps there might be an opportunity to potentially discuss..." Real humans are more direct.
- **Symmetric structure**: AI loves parallel construction. Real emails are messy — one long paragraph, then a one-liner, then bullets.
- **Generic empathy**: "I completely understand how frustrating that must be" without referencing the specific frustration.
- **Excessive formality**: AI defaults to a slightly stiff professional register. Most real workplace emails are more casual.
- **Closing formula stacking**: "Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to hearing from you. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions." Pick one. One.
- **No contractions**: Real emails use "don't", "can't", "we'll". AI often writes "do not", "cannot", "we will."
- **Perfect grammar throughout**: Real emails have occasional fragments, dashes used loosely, sentences starting with "And" or "But."
- **Subject lines that are too clever**: AI-generated subject lines often try too hard. "Unlocking Synergies: A Proposal for Cross-Functional Collaboration" vs. a human's "Idea: joint project with marketing."

### Making AI-assisted emails sound human
- Read the email out loud. If you wouldn't say it that way to the person, rewrite it.
- Add one specific, concrete detail that only you would know.
- Break at least one "rule" — a fragment, an aside, a casual aside in parentheses.
- Use the recipient's first name naturally, not just in the greeting.
- Match the length and formality of recent emails in the thread.
